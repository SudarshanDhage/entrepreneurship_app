## Building Products: A Framework

- **Source:** The Year of the Looking Glass (Medium)
- **Author:** Julie Zhuo (Former VP of Product Design at Facebook)
- **URL:** https://medium.com/the-year-of-the-looking-glass/building-products-91aa93bea4bb
- **Date:** 2025
- **Reading Time:** 7 minutes
- **Key Concepts:** Product development, problem framing, execution, measuring success, metrics, team dynamics, product management, hypothesis testing, product-market fit
- **Key Insight:** Comprehensive framework for building great products based on lessons from Facebook. Four main areas: (1) On Framing: Product succeeds because it solves problem for people - single most important thing. First step: understand what problem to solve and for whom, crystal clear before thinking about solutions. Second question: why is this problem worth solving? Narrowly-defined audience (that you're part of) = can rely on intuition; otherwise rely on research/data. Startups: easier to go after narrowly-defined audience first, expand later. Problem should be easy to communicate in sentence or two, resonate with target audience. (2) Execution: Good execution = getting to believable conclusions in shortest time. Bad execution = fail without learning why, or take year to learn what could be learned in 3 months. What separates teams isn't whether they fail (guaranteed) but how well they consistently execute. Go broad before deep - brainstorm 10, 20, 50 solutions before picking winner. First 5 ideas are obvious; creativity in 11th, 20th, 50th idea. If someone asks "have you considered X?" and answer is "No" = red flag exploration not rigorous enough. Use empirical evidence to narrow down ideas. Frame solution as hypothesis. Constantly look for ways to short-circuit vetting hypothesis (run idea by people, surveys, quick versions). Positive signal on hypothesis doesn't mean rush to ship - separate decision on bar for full launch vs. test. Split big projects into smaller, independently testable milestones. Do team post-mortem after every project. (3) Measuring Success: How you measure success is critical - people rally around it. Define success metrics before launch (avoid confirmation bias). For each success metric, come up with counter metric to avoid plugging one hole with another. If metric moves unexpectedly, first question should be "why?" Use Crystal Ball technique: "If I could know anything about how people use product, what would tell me if it's successful?" Work backwards from abstract answer to measurable metric. Goals should be set with best current information - adjust if new information changes understanding. If don't understand/agree with how team measures success, bring up right away. Constant debates about product direction = probably disagreement in measuring success. For product-market fit, goal on retention rather than engagement or # of users. (4) Team Dynamics: Think in terms of "what helps team be successful" not role expectations. Teams that fall in love with problem > teams that fall in love with particular solutions. Always assume best intentions. Understand what you're uniquely good at and what teammates are good at - divvy up responsibilities accordingly. Good communication key - everyone should feel safe expressing viewpoints, even contrarian. Diversity of opinions = best results.

## Effective Product Management

- **Source:** Medium
- **Author:** Craig McLuckie (VP of Product Management, VMware)
- **URL:** https://cmcluck.medium.com/effective-product-management-8021831a184
- **Date:** 2025
- **Reading Time:** 13 minutes
- **Key Concepts:** Product management discipline, business centricity, technical versatility, customer empathy, collaboration, storytelling, courage, positivity, relentlessness, discipline, influence without authority, product-engineering relationship, anti-patterns
- **Key Insight:** Comprehensive guide to effective product management from VP perspective. Core philosophy: Product management's primary allegiance is to the business - engineering builds technology, product management ensures it generates sustainable business. "PMs as CEOs" mindset can be detrimental in large orgs - PMs don't have CEO empowerment, role is influence not direct authority. Great PMs represent people not in room, light up teams, excellent storytellers who connect teams viscerally to problems. Key virtues: (1) Business centricity - bridge to business, understand sales/financials (top line + bottom line), balance risk/reward, know when something isn't aligned. (2) Technical versatility - understand product deeply, have in-depth customer tech conversations, appreciate engineering practices. "Not getting the tech is like being a barista that doesn't drink coffee." Install and use your technology. (3) Customer empathy - channel customer in hundreds of decisions, big difference between prospective user vs buyer, serve both, beware survivor bias. (4) Collaboration - no team is island, local optimum ≠ global optimum, avoid zero-sum thinking, key elements: building trust, clear communication, honoring trust. (5) Storytelling/Communications - every PM is leader, create sense of potential before it exists, inward-facing critical (get engineers to understand customer problem/business imperatives), manage signal-to-noise ratio ("fog of war" is enemy), think "who needs to know?". (6) Courage/Positivity/Relentlessness - courage: make decisions, accept downside, say no (harder than yes). Positivity: focus on opportunity, remove heat. Relentlessness: 3 steps forward, 2 steps back world. (7) Discipline - grind quotient exists, be reliable, disciplined about priorities. What works well: Knowledge is primary currency (influence without authority), know when spinning wheels and get help, embrace kind directness (Radical Candor), shared fate mindset (one team with engineering), learning mindset (learning machine, celebrate bumps), see bigger machine (willing to cut own product if doesn't fit bigger story). Anti-patterns: "That is beneath me" (ego useless, nothing beneath PM, but don't make it your job), wallpapering over organizational gaps (hiding problems from org), disagree then dig in (disagree+commit but re-examine with data, frame as experiment), passive aggressive PM/eng death spiral (perfect PRD useless if unread, classic sign: quick features get universe-estimates), being Mary Poppins to engineering (PM is strategy/business function, not execution, healthy tension not insulation), saying yes to everything (product defined more by nos than yeses, be practical and call your shot).

## How to Develop Product Sense

- **Source:** Lenny's Newsletter
- **Author:** Jules Walter (Guest author; Product Leader at Google Gemini, previously at YouTube and Slack)
- **URL:** https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/product-sense
- **Date:** 2025
- **Key Concepts:** Product sense, PM skills, empathy, creativity, user research, product observation, product deconstruction, learning from product thinkers, technology trends, user behavior
- **Key Insight:** Comprehensive practical guide to developing product sense, which is the skill of consistently crafting products (or making changes) that have the intended impact on users. Product sense relies on empathy (discover meaningful user needs) and creativity (solutions that effectively address those needs). Four practices: (1) Observe people interacting with products - repeatedly observe people using products (yours or others), attend user research sessions, pay attention to micro details, ask open-ended questions. Key questions: "What do you think the purpose of this product is?", "What actions do you want to take?", "What are you thinking right now? How does that make you feel?". Five lessons: people are time-crunched/distracted (pick right defaults, make primary actions obvious), drop out when confused/nervous (unambiguous labels), don't give too much info at once (Slack pricing example - dropdowns increased purchases), context impacts decisions (comparisons, social proof), make goals/actions clear (Slack onboarding experiment increased retention). (2) Deconstruct everyday products - observe yourself using products (1-2 hours/month), use questions from Julie Zhuo's product critique framework, compare products in same category (Cash App vs Venmo example - different strategies), observe own reactions (frustration and delight moments), meditation helps improve observation skills. (3) Learn from great product thinkers - attend product reviews, take notes, look for patterns in questions/feedback, understand mental checklists and principles. Example: Stewart Butterfield at Slack - "The details are not the details. They make the design." (rooster @channel warning example), understand problems and frame them with strong constraints (streamlines decision-making). If don't work at company with great thinkers: find external mentors, read articles/interviews (Julie Zhuo, Stewart Butterfield, David Lieb, Rahul Vohra), ask questions about prompts, decision points, alternative approaches, surprising insights, principles/frameworks. (4) Be curious about technology and domain - learn about emerging trends (technology, society, regulations). Examples: Cash App + Bitcoin (76% revenue), Uber (smartphones/GPS/Google Maps APIs), Clubhouse (pandemic). Track macro trends (platform shifts - web3/AR/VR/AI, social shifts - remote work, regulations - privacy laws) and micro changes (Figma + WebGL example). Keep open mind, ask "If this trend reaches full potential, what opportunities will it create?" Keep up via developer conferences, industry analysts (Ben Thompson), Twitter (Naval, Elad Gil, Balaji), invest/advise startups or follow top VCs. Meet engineers/domain experts for micro changes. As Paul Graham said: "Live in the future, then build what's missing." Signs of improvement: notice subtle things (micro frustrations/delights), anticipate non-obvious problems, develop higher-quality hypotheses, contribute unique insights, be right more often about impact on metrics, get positive feedback. Additional practice: actively reflect on product sense loop (identify assumptions, construct experiments, analyze outcomes, reflect on right/wrong, repeat). If assumptions proven more frequently (e.g., 2/10 to 8/10), product sense improving. Resources: Tony Fadell TED talk, Marty Cagan's "Inspired", Don Norman course.

## Musk's 5-Step Design Process

- **Source:** ModelThinkers
- **Author:** Elon Musk (via interview with Everyday Astronaut)
- **URL:** https://modelthinkers.com/mental-model/musks-5-step-design-process
- **Date:** Unknown (interview date)
- **Key Concepts:** Design process, engineering efficiency, requirements, simplification, optimization, cycle time, automation, first principles, lean thinking
- **Key Insight:** Systematic 5-step design process that SpaceX aims to "apply rigorously" for efficient engineering and product development. The 5 steps (must be done in order): (1) Make the requirements less dumb - "your requirements are definitely dumb, it does not matter who gave them to you." Question the question. Requirements from "smart person" often most dangerous because you might not question them enough. Similar to Framestorming. (2) Delete the part or process - "If you're not adding things back in at least 10% of the time, you're clearly not deleting enough." Start lean, build up when required. Bias is to add things "in case" but you can make "in case" arguments for many things. Each requirement/constraint must be accountable to person, not department. Example: SpaceX grid fins didn't need to fold back - eliminated need for massive mechanisms. (3) Simplify or optimize the design - "Possibly the most common error of a smart engineer is to optimize a thing that should not exist." Must work through first two steps before optimizing. Engineers need holistic view. Example: Engineers invested in reducing rocket engine weight but hadn't addressed reducing payload weight equivalently. (4) Accelerate cycle time - "If you're digging your grave, don't dig faster." Embrace speed but only after steps 1-3 ensure right direction. Accelerated Agile approach, but only after first three steps satisfied. (5) Automate - Don't automate before earlier points addressed. Tesla Model 3 battery mat story: invested massive time/effort to automate process before asking what mat was for - discovered it was no longer required (created to reduce sound but unnecessary). Order is critical - must work through steps 1-3 before accelerating/automating. Can be applied to UX, marketing campaigns, product design. Reflects First Principle Thinking, Framestorming, Occam's Razor, Lean Startup/MVP (Musk refers to "Minimum Viable Rocket"). Rejects adding features "in case." Limitations: May need to cycle back and forth in complex applications as requirements are uncovered or refined.

## As a User, I Don't Want To (Inverted User Stories)

- **Source:** UX Collective (Medium)
- **Author:** Pavel Samsonov
- **URL:** https://uxdesign.cc/as-a-user-i-dont-want-to-973990a30158
- **Date:** 2025
- **Key Concepts:** User stories, product development, user experience, value vs cost, product complexity, feature bloat, product management, agile, scrum, innovation
- **Key Insight:** Task-oriented user stories lead to bad product decisions because they mix up value with cost. Original user story format: "As [user] I want [to reach a goal] so that I can [obtain a benefit]" - focused on goal and benefit. Degenerated into task-oriented: "As [user] I want [to do a task] so that I can [use the tool]" - focuses on work. Examples of corrupted stories: "I want to click the button so that I can see the page," "I want to enter my email so that I can receive exclusive offers," "As a user, I want to log in" (missing "so that"). The truth: users don't want to use a product at all - ideal experience is reaching goal without doing any work. Original format outlined benefit users might pay for, team figures out how to provide benefit and costs. But tasks, tools, and features are NOT benefits - they are costs users pay to achieve goal. Adding features degrades experience by increasing cost. With physical products, users have way out when complexity exceeds value. With software, they just leave. Task-oriented stories obfuscate lack of value - "as a user, I want to fill out the form" fools team into thinking user will give product something for nothing. Cost is hidden because user is defined as someone who wants to fill out forms, even if no such person exists. With stories like that, every ticket becomes self-justifying. Features multiply, complexity rises, value plummets, but teams can't see it. Solution: Invert the user story. Task-oriented story contains useful information: what it calls benefit is actually the cost, what it calls goal is solution hypothesis. Example: "As a user, I want to customize my toolbar so that I can optimize my browsing experience" becomes "As a user, I want to optimize my browsing experience without having to customize my toolbar." This shifts focus from task to desired outcome, highlights feature as hypothesis needing validation, encourages innovation, reduces complexity. Working backwards: Task-oriented stories capture existing patterns that are obvious - if benefit brings obvious feature to mind, it probably exists elsewhere as cost. Existing cost is great place to find pain points. Amazon approach: "today, customers have to..." - "Today, customers have to customize the toolbar if they want to improve their browsing experience" sounds like struggle, not feature to build. Inverting stories turns rush to add ticket into opportunity to innovate and differentiate. Key takeaways: Original focused on goals/benefits, task-oriented focuses on work/tools, tasks/features are costs not benefits, invert by changing "I want to do X" to "I want Y without having to do X," reveals real goal and highlights assumptions, opens better solution space.

## Startups Don't Need Product Managers Who Are Visionaries (Street Sweeper PM)

- **Source:** VentureBeat
- **Author:** Jon Sadow (Cofounder and Chief Product Officer at Scoop; previously at Google)
- **URL:** https://venturebeat.com/entrepreneur/startups-dont-need-product-managers-who-are-visionaries
- **Date:** 2025
- **Key Concepts:** Product management, startup PM, visionary vs facilitator, street sweeper, prioritization, MVP, embracing what you don't know, team relationships, hiring product managers
- **Key Insight:** Counterintuitive perspective: startups don't need product managers striving to be visionaries. Maximum benefit comes from someone who focuses less on glamour/invention and more on empowering teams to build most impactful product possible. Core of great PM: clearing path for everyone else on team to set them up for success. Four key practices: (1) Be a street sweeper - focus on mundane but critical tasks. Example: International product rollout at Google required focusing on finance law, translation services, font size details for different languages rather than exciting international travel. Without mundane conversations about font size or finance law, product never would have launched. When managers dream up next great product, they often aren't addressing mundane but critical tasks. Refusal to sweep street (or belief they're above it) leads to ineffective processes, delayed development/deployment. (2) Prioritize, prioritize, prioritize - example of feature development where team initially wanted full vision with bells and whistles. Realized needed street sweeper mentality: clear road of complicated capabilities, prioritize simplest version. Visionary approach would never have launched - would prioritize idea rather than MVP. Visionary PMs drawn to revolutionary/idyllic rather than evolutionary. Must be comfortable asking what's most important, prioritizing necessary less-flashy capabilities, accepting iterative rollout/slimmer product. (3) Embrace what you don't know - PMs come from technical or non-technical backgrounds, can polarize dissonance. Example: Technical PM writing code instead of facilitating slowed development, poor resource use, suggests air of knowing better. Visionary PM might feel they have all answers. How well manager fills knowledge gaps matters more. Must surround with experts in designing, building, marketing, distributing. Embrace their knowledge, don't make decisions beyond expertise, avoid leaning too heavily on familiar. (4) Build mutually respectful relationships - mentor advice: PMs should [almost] always let everyone else take credit while they take blame. Not just to make people feel good - when PM expends energy showing visionary greatness, damages team morale/motivation. Visionary PMs who don't sweep streets get in way of team efforts. Managers dreaming up idyllic vision slow development, silence other voices. Shows process is about catering to PM's whims/ego rather than building right product. Must give teammates respect, show appreciation, make clear success tied to team success. In practice, team actually deserves credit (PM didn't write code, make designs, create marketing). Once team understands PM support, they return trust/support. Visionary PMs influence development cycle in ways detrimental to process. Startups need someone who: appreciates street sweeper role, prioritizes well, embraces what they don't know, invests in relationships. Building great products is about iterating and supporting smooth development process. For leadership hiring PM: ask what candidate thinks their job will be. Do they see themselves sitting in back room thinking of next big thing? Or as facilitator? Answer is telling - right approach engenders sustained success.

## GitLab Product Principles

- **Source:** GitLab Handbook
- **Author:** GitLab (Company-wide product principles)
- **URL:** https://handbook.gitlab.com/handbook/product/product-principles/
- **Date:** 2025)
- **Key Concepts:** Product principles, customer-centric innovation, customer zero, design-led, quality over velocity, data-driven, iteration, MVC (Minimal Valuable Change), convention over configuration, subtractive thinking, integration principles, product-led growth, feature naming, next-generation development, user experience, ambition, product usage, flow one, small primitives, feature ownership
- **Key Insight:** Comprehensive set of product principles for building world-class products through customer-centric innovation. Eight core principles: (1) We are customer zero - use our own product, challenge "why" if feature isn't something Engineering would use. (2) We are not our only customer - meet with customers, validate assumptions through user research/interviews/feedback. (3) We are design-led - UX is top priority, technical product should be easy for someone new to get started. (4) We value quality over velocity - everything must be user validated, bug free, secure, scalable, documented, available on all deployments. No technical debt in favor of velocity. (5) We value data over intuition - measure outcomes not launches, all features instrumented for success metrics. (6) We fail fast and iterate with intention - define hypothesis, validate through problem validation, build iteration strategy, revalidate through solution validation. (7) Product-led growth - product should be best Sales team member, enable feature discovery moments. (8) We win as a team - highest execution and collaboration across Product and cross-functional teams. Key frameworks: Minimal Valuable Change (MVC) - smallest measurable improvement with four pillars: relentless customer focus, measurable outcomes, product functionality adhering to GA criteria, future vision. MVC means reducing scope to ship quickly, not shipping something that hurts usability. First impressions important. Examples: ship via API before UI, expose minimal functionality. Not advised for core experiences. Iteration - break problems down small, focus on reduced cycle time. Each iteration must define expected impact and establish metrics for success. Success measured by achieving objectives, not deployment. Subtractive thinking - humans favor adding features, PMs should recognize bias and remove features when more efficient. Convention over Configuration - reduce complexity while offering needed features. Working by default (enabled + setup by default). Avoid limits, avoid configuration when possible. Configuration principles: builds up over time, hard to remove, expensive testing mechanism (use feature flags instead). Avoid "Not Invented Here" - leverage existing technology to get to market quickly, contribute to Open Source, focus on making GitLab better. Integrate thoughtfully - don't clone everything, prevent broken workflows for free, charge for adding value, respect security/permissions, use empty states for discovery, use own design language, be transparent about data source. Avoid plugins/marketplaces - open core allows adding directly to codebase, works out of box, tested together, no upgrade fears. Product naming - descriptive not distinctive names (efficient, inclusive), use prepositions for third-party integrations. Next Generation: Modern first (modern teams before legacy), Developer first (start with developer then other personas), Cloud-native first (then support rest), Prioritize current adopters (optimize for current/future adopters over early adopters). UX principles: Strive for simplicity, Depth over breadth, Better than before (MVC not better than nothing), Timeless design. Be Ambitious - accomplish "impossible" things, velocity unchanged whether scheduled ambitiously or with slack. Drive Product Usage - AARRR framework (single feature), Customer Adoption Journey (cross-adoption). Golden Journey: Create → Plan → Verify → Release. Flow One - workflow of MVCs for specific narrow use-case, avoid inflexible long-term plan. Additional: Avoid enforced workflows (allow enterprise flexibility at group level, default off), Prefer small primitives (building blocks, can be combined), Every feature owned by group (unowned = unsupervised, accrues debt), Always allow deploying to production (safeguards removable in emergency), Data-driven work (tools for both SaaS/self-managed, start from question).

## How to Hire a Product Manager

- **Source:** Bring the Donuts (Ken Norton's Blog)
- **Author:** Ken Norton (Executive Coach, Former Google Product Leader)
- **URL:** https://www.bringthedonuts.com/essays/productmanager.html
- **Date:** 2025, updated 2023
- **Key Concepts:** Product management, hiring product managers, PM skills, product instincts, technical background, leadership, shipping products, startup hiring, product management career
- **Key Insight:** Classic essay that defined the product manager role and launched thousands of PM careers. Reality: Product management may be the one job organization would get along fine without (at least for a while). Without engineers, nothing built; without sales, nothing sold; without designers, product looks like crap. But without PMs, everyone fills in gap and goes on. Important reminder: as PM, you're expendable. In long run great product management makes difference between winning/losing, but you have to prove it. Product management is weird discipline full of oddballs and rejects that never quite fit in anywhere else - combines elements of engineering, design, marketing, sales, business development. Six key characteristics: (1) Hire all the smart people - most importantly, raw intellectual horsepower. Take wickedly smart inexperienced PM over average intellect with years of experience. Product management is about thinking on feet, staying ahead of competitors, projecting into minds of colleagues/customers. Ask analytical questions until sure candidate is smarter than you. Best candidates bounce out of chairs with excitement when posed theoretical problems. Update 2023: Don't ask brainteaser/logic puzzle questions - move toward structured interviews with work samples and situational problems without "right answer." (2) Strong technical background - not necessarily CS degree, but favor people who've been in technical roles. Gives ability to relate to engineers and grasp technical details. Technical PMs more successful conveying requirements to engineers and relaying details to non-technical colleagues/customers. Critical pitfall: PM who's former engineer needs to realize they're FORMER engineer. PMs who still try to take charge of technical decisions/implementation details will crash spectacularly. Prefer technical people who've already made move to product management at previous job - they've gone through adaptation period. Interview questions: Why move from engineering to PM? Biggest advantage/disadvantage of technical background? Biggest lesson learned? What wish you'd known as engineer? How earn respect of engineering team? (3) "Spidey-sense" product instincts and creativity - highly subjective, difficult to evaluate, extraordinarily important. Certain people born with innate product instincts - just know what makes great product, instincts usually point right direction, passionate advocates. Small decisions where great PM distances from decent one. Know they've got spidey-sense when suggest approaches nobody thought of but immediately strike everyone as obvious. Evaluating in interview: check if candidate (a) independently echoed your concerns about product, (b) taught you something new about product (shows not afraid to speak critically, probably smarter), (c) turned you on to something new and interesting (people with great instincts notice great products before everyone else). Interview questions: Tell me about great product you've encountered recently (drives crazy when candidates name your products). What made [iPod/eBay] successful? What do you dislike about my product? How improve it? What problems will we encounter in 1/2/10 years? How know product well designed? Best/worst ideas ever had? How know when to cut corners? Lessons about UI design? How decide what not to build? Biggest product mistake? Least interesting aspects of PM? Consider yourself creative? Update 2023: Product designers ascended to "third leg of stool" - include product designers in interview loop. (4) Leadership that's earned - PMs usually leaders but don't have direct line authority, earn authority and lead by influence. Leadership and interpersonal skills critical. Reference checks most effective way to measure, especially peers/individual contributors who worked with but didn't report to candidate. Interview questions: Is consensus always good? Difference between management and leadership? Kinds of people like/difficult to work with? Time team didn't gel? How get team to commit to schedule? What lose your confidence? Manage people from different functions differently? Learned about saying no? Ultimate accountability for shipping? Team let you down and you took blame? Tolerance for mistakes changed? Good news or bad news first? Approach to hiring? (5) Ability to channel multiple points-of-view - requires wearing multiple hats, advocate for whoever isn't in room (customer, engineering, sales, executives, marketing). Capable of doing other people's jobs but smart enough to know not to. Play devil's advocate, unsatisfied with simple answers. Evaluating: get lots of people in interview process. At minimum, representatives from engineering, design, marketing. Can grow to pre-sales engineering, support, developer relations, business development, legal, customers. One carefully selected representative of each key function. Nobody judges understanding of sales process like salesperson. Give specific instructions to interviewers. Interview questions: How learned to work with sales? Best way to interface with customers? What makes marketing tick? How know when design on right track? How PM support business development? Learned about managing up? Best way to work with executives? (6) Give me someone who's shipped something - unless position very junior, hire PMs who've actually shipped product (start to finish, concept to launch). Nothing better indication of ability to ship great products than having done it before. Past performance indicates future success. Check references with manager and engineering/sales/marketing counterparts. Note: Rules ordered for reason - take brilliantly smart PM over dimmer experienced one even if former hasn't shipped. Startup vs Big Company: Startup needs strong generalists who adapt (everyone does little of everything, hard to predict future). Big company has specific roles (lower likelihood of change). Deadly thinking at startup: "seems suited for this very specific role" - works at big company, deadly at startup.
