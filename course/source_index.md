### Founder Mode

- **Source:** Paul Graham's Essays
- **Author:** Paul Graham
- **URL:** https://paulgraham.com/foundermode.html
- **Date:** September 2024
- **Featured:** Paul Graham, Brian Chesky (Airbnb), Steve Jobs (Apple), Ron Conway
- **Key Concepts:** Founder mode vs manager mode, scaling startups, conventional management advice, "hire good people and give them room", professional managers vs founders, skip-level meetings, gaslighting founders, modular design management, Steve Jobs annual retreats, delegation boundaries, Airbnb free cash flow margin
- **Key Insight:** Two different ways to run companies: founder mode vs manager mode. Conventional advice ("hire good people and give them room") is for professional managers, not founders. Scaling doesn't have to mean switching to manager mode. Founders feel gaslit by conventional advice and employees. Skip-level engagement will be normal in founder mode. More complex than manager mode but works better.
- **Examples:** Brian Chesky (Airbnb) following conventional advice led to disastrous results, then studied Steve Jobs and improved; Steve Jobs annual retreats for 100 most important people (not highest on org chart); many successful YC founders had same experience
- **Used In:**

### What to Do When Growth Stalls

- **Source:** Andrew Chen Blog
- **Author:** Andrew Chen (Partner at Andreessen Horowitz)
- **URL:** https://andrewchen.com/growth-stalls/
- **Featured:** Andrew Chen, @bokardo (Next Feature Fallacy)
- **Key Concepts:** Growth stall diagnosis, crisis arrives slowly then all at once, churn catching up with acquisition, retention as baseline assessment, admitting people don't want your product, positioning against existing categories, activation vs retention, network effects (smile curve), next feature fallacy, top of funnel problems, repeatable growth strategies vs lightning strikes, growth loops (viral, UGC SEO), polishing UX flows for growth, signup flows, invite flows, payment flows
- **Key Insight:** A product stalls when its churn catches up with its customer acquisition. You can analyze growth stalls systematically by asking questions, gathering data, and assessing the stall to zero in on problems. Start with retention benchmarks. "My product is not retaining" is often a fancy phrase for "people don't want to use my product." Moving retention numbers is the hardest thing to moveâ€”initial numbers are usually a ceiling. Strong retention with flat growth is actually a good thingâ€”just need more channels.
- **What NOT to Do:** "This next magic feature will fix all our growth problems" (PM), "We need to spend more money on marketing" (Marketer), "Have you considered adding more AI?" (Investor)
- **Retention Benchmarks:** Social app: D1/D7/D30 of 60/30/15%, DAU/MAU over 20%, SMB subscription churn <5%, marketplace supply side >50% YoY retention
- **Two Exceptions for Retention:** (1) Better initial user activation improves long-term retention (first 7 days matter most), (2) Network effects products can have "smile curve" where retention goes up as network fills in
- **Next Feature Fallacy:** The fallacy that the next feature you add will suddenly make people want to use the entire product. Need to listen to "silent majority" who churn, not hardcore users who stay.
- **Growth Loops:** Viral loops (scalable, free), UGC SEO-driven loops (Yelp, Reddit, Glassdoor). Growth loops scale with user base size, keeping up with churn.
- **UX Flows That Matter:** Signup flows (20-50% conversion gains), invite/referral flows (viral factor), payment flows (harvest purchase intent early). These are unsexy but critical.
- **Examples:** Facebook stalled at colleges, then internationally, then before mobile. Dropbox needed referral system and shared folders. TikTok stalled as dance video platform before paid marketing push.

### Superlinear Returns

- **Source:** Paul Graham's Essays
- **Author:** Paul Graham
- **URL:** https://paulgraham.com/superlinear.html
- **Date:** October 2023
- **Featured:** Paul Graham, Newton, Durer, Darwin
- **Key Concepts:** Superlinear returns, exponential growth, thresholds, winner take all, learning compounds, work compounds, always be learning, follow curiosity not careerism, democratization of opportunity, doing exceptionally good work, fields with superlinear returns, inequality and superlinear returns, independent-mindedness, do things that don't scale, don't equate work with a job
- **Key Insight:** Returns for performance are superlinear, not linear. If your product is only half as good as your competitor's, you don't get half as many customersâ€”you get no customers. Two fundamental causes: exponential growth (when how well you do depends on how well you've done) and thresholds (winner take all). They often combine. Most important case: learningâ€”knowledge grows exponentially and has thresholds. Fields with superlinear returns: sports, politics, art, music, acting, directing, writing, math, science, starting companies, investing. When excluding sports/politics/fame effects, these are exactly the fields requiring independent-mindednessâ€”where ideas must be novel, not just correct.
- **How to Find Superlinear Returns:** (1) Seek work that compoundsâ€”directly (building infrastructure, audience) or by teaching you (learning compounds), (2) Always be learningâ€”if you're not learning, you're probably not on path to superlinear returns, (3) Follow curiosity not careerismâ€”promising questions seem mystifying but perhaps unimportant, (4) Replace mediocre yet popular things, (5) Look for fields where a few big winners outperform everyone else
- **The Territory Is Expanding:** Progress in technology and decreasing importance of organizations is democratizing opportunity. World dominated by organizations damped variation in returns for performance, but this has eroded. More people can have freedom artists/writers had. More variation in outcomesâ€”those who do well do even better, those who do badly do worse. Not for everyoneâ€”most better off as part of pool. For ambitious people who know they're good enough or can afford to risk trying.
- **How to Take Advantage:** Do exceptionally good workâ€”at far end of curve, incremental effort is a bargain, less competition because few even try. Recipe: Choose work with natural aptitude and deep interest, work on own projects, work hard without burning out, reach frontiers of knowledge, notice gaps, explore them, take risks, seek best colleagues, develop good taste, be honest with yourself, exercise/sleep/eat well, follow curiosity. Take multiple shotsâ€”luck matters more when working independently, start taking risks early.
- **Key Lessons:** Superlinear returns seem small at firstâ€”put in initial effort anyway because reward curve rises steeply at far end (this is "do things that don't scale" in startups). Don't equate work with a jobâ€”work is what you carry with you, part of portfolio. If you follow curiosity far enough, you'll end up in field with superlinear returns. Territory not staticâ€”most extreme returns come from expanding it. Curiosity may be more powerful than ambitionâ€”ambition climbs existing peaks, curiosity may find questions that grow into mountains beneath you.
- **Superlinear Returns Imply Inequality:** Steeper return curve = greater variation in outcomes. This is feature of world, not flaw. Correlation so strong it's heuristicâ€”look for fields where few big winners outperform everyone else.

### Patents and IP Strategy: A Counterpoint

- **Source:** Guy Kawasaki's Blog
- **Author:** J. Matthew Buchanan, Douglas J. Sorocco, and Stephen M. Nipper (Rethink(IP))
- **URL:** https://guykawasaki.com/counterpoint_pa/
- **Date:** 2025
- **Featured:** Guy Kawasaki, patent attorneys at Rethink(IP)
- **Key Concepts:** Patent law, intellectual property strategy, trade secrets, defensive publications, trademarks, domain names, branding strategy, invention management, eBay vs. MercExchange
- **Key Insight:** Counterpoint to Guy Kawasaki's piece on defensibility. Patent law is currently a sea of chaos, and patents don't pack the oomph they once did, particularly for startups. eBay vs. MercExchange changed value of patents for small companies. Application backlog at PTO is all-time highâ€”average 30+ months to issue, 5+ years in backlogged tech areas, delays up to 10+ years. Tech startups can't count on patents to shut down Microsofts of the world. BUT patents still play important roleâ€”just not the whole enchilada. Need comprehensive IP strategy, not knee-jerk "Patent Everything!" or "Patent Nothing!"
- **Put Patents in Right Place:** Ask "Should we patent?" at end of invention management. Consider: (1) How competitors using patents? (Biotech/pharma/medical devicesâ€”can't get past Go without them), (2) Better as trade secret? (Coca-Cola formula exampleâ€”need to maintain secrecy in-house, competitors can't reverse engineer. Drawback: competitor later patents same thing = no protection), (3) Make publicly available? (Source code/APIâ€”community support, critical mass), (4) Defensive publication? (Donate to public domain to prevent competitors from patentingâ€”IBM Technical Disclosure Bulletin example. Once released, genie can't go back)
- **Value Beyond Litigation:** Patents add value beyond ability to sue: (1) Selling point ("patented technology"), (2) Block competitors from getting patents (keeps them from suing you), (3) Counter-offensive weapons (counter-sue if sued, threat can prevent suit or force settlement), (4) Bluff smaller competitors (nuisance value settlement cheaper than court battle), (5) Portfolio for acquirers (who can afford to sue)
- **Inventions First, Patents Second:** Think about role of inventions in business firstâ€”are they important component of value and growth? If so, develop efficient system: invention disclosure forms, processing systems, regular review meetings, annual cataloging with decision-makers
- **Trademarks and Branding:** Get trademarks that are strong and protectable. Check domain name availabilityâ€”many don't spend enough time on this. Get the domain nameâ€”can't afford to lose this race. Get typos and variant domains too ($9 insurance vs. thousands in legal fees laterâ€”Utube.com vs. Youtube.com example). Develop overall branding strategy including domain name. Branding as important as technology. Don't wait too longâ€”sucks to rebrand 20 months in because didn't get legal opinion. Solid branding with appropriate protections creates significant value. Example: How great would it be to call your search company "Google"? You can't.
- **Bottom Line:** If patent attorney tells you to patent everything without regard to bigger picture, find new one. Good patent attorney looks at bigger picture and proposes strategy, not disparate tactics. Patents aren't end-all answer to defensibility. "We've got tons and tons of patents!" alone looks clueless. BUT strong IP strategy designed in context of relevant industry CAN give you defensibility.

### Avoiding the Wrong MVP Approach

- **Date:** 2025
- **Featured:** Jared M. Spool
- **Key Concepts:** MVP, minimum viable product, minimum viable experiment, product development, assumption validation
- **Key Insight:** MVPs often don't need code, but teams forget this. Organizations are so used to solving every problem with software that they forget they can learn what they need by faster, more efficient means. The minimum viable experiment asks: "What's the smallest test we can run to validate our riskiest assumption?" Before writing code, ask: (1) What's the riskiest assumption we're making? (2) What's the smallest test we can run to validate it? (3) Does that test require code, or can we learn faster without it? Example: Insurance company wanted customers to take photos instead of sending photographers. Instead of building an app, first test if customer photos are adequateâ€”ask customers to email photos and see if adjusters can use them. Some teams use "minimum viable experiment" instead of MVP to differentiate from the bias that code = product. If you can learn without building, do that first. Code comes later, after you've validated that the idea actually works.

### Signs You Aren't Really Building a Minimum Viable Product

- **Source:** Anthony Panozzo's Blog

- **Date:** 2025
- **Featured:** Anthony Panozzo
- **Referenced Works:** Eric Ries' *The Lean Startup*
- **Key Concepts:** MVP, minimum viable product, validated learning, build-measure-learn loop, assumption validation, success criteria
- **Key Insight:** Most so-called "MVPs" are not really MVPs because they are not focused on the process of learning, and as a result, wasteful. MVP is not a minimal product, it is a strategy and process directed toward making and selling a product to customers. "The minimum viable product is that version of a new product which allows a team to collect the maximum amount of validated learning about customers with the least effort." MVPs can be many things: landing pages, paper prototypes, pretending to be a magical algorithm, going to bus stops to test carpool ideas, etc. Concerns when MVP term is used loosely: (1) little emphasis on what assumptions the MVP seeks to validate, (2) no clear success or failure criteria, (3) there might be an easier way to learn. The build-measure-learn (BML) loop should be thought through in reverseâ€”first focus on what we want to learn, then how we'll measure it, then how we should build. Any additional work beyond what was required to start learning is waste. If you can't answer three questions, you're not building a real MVP: (1) What are you trying to learn? (2) What data are you collecting? (3) What determines success or failure? Without learning, you're not going through the feedback loopâ€”just building version 1.0.

Early adopters are not price-sensitive - they care about benefits above all else, want to beat competition, willing to take risks. ### The Sequoia Guide to Pricing

- **Source:** Sequoia Capital Articles
- **Author:** Sequoia Capital
- **URL:** https://articles.sequoiacap.com/pricing-your-product
- **Date:** Published on Sequoia Capital Articles
- **Key Concepts:** Perceived value, pricing psychology, decoy pricing, pinch points, snap judgments, horizontal/vertical assortment, endowment effect, pricing hypothesis
- **Key Insight:** Comprehensive guide to pricing that emphasizes pricing is as much art as science, relying on marketing and psychology as much as classical economics. Core thesis: "Pricing is not a math problem, it's a judgment problem." Key concepts: (1) Increase perceived value - focus on gap between price and perceived value, not just cost vs price. If nobody's buying, it's because gap between price and perceived value doesn't exist or isn't large enough. (2) Let price tell a story - price serves as proxy for quality ("Premium pricing communicates a premium product" - Natera example). Where you display price matters. ### RICE: Simple Prioritization for Product Managers

- **Source:** Intercom Blog
- **Author:** Sean McBride
- **URL:** https://www.intercom.com/blog/rice-simple-prioritization-for-product-managers/
- **Date:** 2025
- **Reading Time:** 7 minutes
- **Key Concepts:** Product prioritization, RICE framework, roadmap planning, product management, reach, impact, confidence, effort, scoring systems
- **Key Insight:** RICE is a scoring framework for prioritizing product work that combines four factors: Reach (how many people will this impact per time period), Impact (how much will this impact each person - 3=massive, 2=high, 1=medium, 0.5=low, 0.25=minimal), Confidence (how confident are you in estimates - 100%=high, 80%=medium, 50%=low), and Effort (person-months required). Formula: RICE Score = (Reach Ã— Impact Ã— Confidence) / Effort. The resulting score measures "total impact per time worked" - exactly what you want to maximize. Why prioritization is difficult: (1) Satisfying to work on pet ideas you'd use yourself vs projects with broad reach, (2) Tempting to focus on clever ideas vs projects that directly impact goals, (3) Exciting to dive into new ideas vs projects you're confident about, (4) Easy to discount additional effort. Framework helps avoid these biases and consistently compare projects. Key principles: Use real measurements from product metrics for Reach (don't pull numbers from hat), be honest about Confidence (avoid decision paralysis with multiple-choice scale), estimate Effort as person-months (rough estimates with whole numbers or 0.5), Impact uses multiple-choice scale to avoid "tangled mess of gut feeling". After scoring, sort and re-evaluate - reconsider estimates if scores seem wrong, accept gut instinct may be wrong. RICE shouldn't be hard and fast rule - dependencies, table stakes features, or other factors may require working on lower-scored projects first, but framework helps identify when making trade-offs. Framework helps make better-informed decisions and defend them to others. Examples provided for each factor showing how to calculate Reach (450, 2000, 800 customers per quarter), assign Impact scores (3, 1, 2), Confidence scores (100%, 80%, 50%), and Effort scores (2, 4, 1 person-months).

### Effective Product Management

- **Source:** Medium
- **Author:** Craig McLuckie (VP of Product Management, VMware)
- **URL:** https://cmcluck.medium.com/effective-product-management-8021831a184
- **Date:** 2025
- **Reading Time:** 13 minutes
- **Key Concepts:** Product management discipline, business centricity, technical versatility, customer empathy, collaboration, storytelling, courage, positivity, relentlessness, discipline, influence without authority, product-engineering relationship, anti-patterns
- **Key Insight:** Comprehensive guide to effective product management from VP perspective. Core philosophy: Product management's primary allegiance is to the business - engineering builds technology, product management ensures it generates sustainable business. "PMs as CEOs" mindset can be detrimental in large orgs - PMs don't have CEO empowerment, role is influence not direct authority. Great PMs represent people not in room, light up teams, excellent storytellers who connect teams viscerally to problems. Key virtues: (1) Business centricity - bridge to business, understand sales/financials (top line + bottom line), balance risk/reward, know when something isn't aligned. (2) Technical versatility - understand product deeply, have in-depth customer tech conversations, appreciate engineering practices. "Not getting the tech is like being a barista that doesn't drink coffee." Install and use your technology. (3) Customer empathy - channel customer in hundreds of decisions, big difference between prospective user vs buyer, serve both, beware survivor bias. (4) Collaboration - no team is island, local optimum â‰  global optimum, avoid zero-sum thinking, key elements: building trust, clear communication, honoring trust. (5) Storytelling/Communications - every PM is leader, create sense of potential before it exists, inward-facing critical (get engineers to understand customer problem/business imperatives), manage signal-to-noise ratio ("fog of war" is enemy), think "who needs to know?". (6) Courage/Positivity/Relentlessness - courage: make decisions, accept downside, say no (harder than yes). Positivity: focus on opportunity, remove heat. Relentlessness: 3 steps forward, 2 steps back world. (7) Discipline - grind quotient exists, be reliable, disciplined about priorities. What works well: Knowledge is primary currency (influence without authority), know when spinning wheels and get help, embrace kind directness (Radical Candor), shared fate mindset (one team with engineering), learning mindset (learning machine, celebrate bumps), see bigger machine (willing to cut own product if doesn't fit bigger story). Anti-patterns: "That is beneath me" (ego useless, nothing beneath PM, but don't make it your job), wallpapering over organizational gaps (hiding problems from org), disagree then dig in (disagree+commit but re-examine with data, frame as experiment), passive aggressive PM/eng death spiral (perfect PRD useless if unread, classic sign: quick features get universe-estimates), being Mary Poppins to engineering (PM is strategy/business function, not execution, healthy tension not insulation), saying yes to everything (product defined more by nos than yeses, be practical and call your shot).

### Top 10 Reasons for Slow Velocity

- **Source:** SVPG (Silicon Valley Product Group)
- **Author:** Marty Cagan
- **URL:** https://www.svpg.com/top-10-reasons-for-slow-velocity/
- **Date:** 2025
- **Key Concepts:** Product velocity, slow delivery, organizational bottlenecks, product ownership, project management, release cycles, product discovery, UX design, product vision, dedicated teams, technical debt, architecture, team coordination
- **Key Insight:** Diagnostic framework for why product teams move slowly. Most organizations blame developer skills, but root causes are typically organizational/structural: (1) Lack of strong product owners - largest single reason, causes churn and morale issues. (2) Lack of strong project management - second biggest reason, need dedicated PM/ScrumMaster who is "pushy but not bossy" to remove impediments. (3) Infrequent release cycles - need releases no less frequently than every two weeks, requires regression test automation and release automation. (4) Not including lead engineers in discovery - need key participants from day 1 to contribute faster alternative approaches, prevents late-stage churn. (5) Not utilizing UX design in discovery - UX should be completed before items hit backlog, defined "pixel perfect," prevents waiting for designs/assets and "feed the beast." (6) Lack of product vision and focus - rapidly shifting roadmap/priorities cause churn, need clear big picture vision. (7) Lack of dedicated teams - treating engineers like chess pieces prevents expertise building and team relationships needed for velocity. (8) Lack of dedicated rapid response team - same teams doing sustaining/escalations and forward progress causes interruptions, need separate rapid response team. (9) Inflexible architecture/technical debt - architecture doesn't facilitate rapid evolution, must be attacked ongoing (can't fix overnight). (10) Missing holistic view - teams don't understand what others are doing and how parts fit together, causes thrashing/churn due to unclear dependencies/inter-relationships. Good news: each can be addressed. However, velocity less important than releasing good software that makes difference for business.

### 50 Short Product Lessons

- **Source:** Cutlefish Blog
- **Author:** John Cutler
- **URL:** https://cutle.fish/blog/50-product-lessons
- **Date:** 2025
- **Key Concepts:** Product development, bets, opportunities, interventions, premature convergence, missions, projects, starting together, coherence, operating models, data, measurement, chronic vs acute issues, short-term thinking, velocity, storytelling, strategy, prioritization, learning cadence, learned helplessness, feature factories, customer connection, iteration, decision-making, deadlines, forcing functions, dependencies, temporary products, vocabulary, reflection
- **Key Insight:** Comprehensive collection of 50 short, conversational product lessons covering the full spectrum of product development. Key themes include: (1) Thinking in bets - portfolios of bets of different sizes and durations, communicating bets to new team members, avoiding one-size-fits-all approaches. (2) Opportunities vs interventions - framing work as opportunities and temporary interventions rather than permanent problems/solutions, enables incremental value capture. (3) Premature convergence - danger of converging on solutions too early, intuition drives early convergence, right time is "a little later than comfortable," look for period of messiness. (4) Missions vs projects - missions are open-ended, nested, have stopping functions vs predetermined definition of done, longer missions suggest value. (5) Starting together - limit planning inventory, get whole team experiencing problem for first time together (haunted house analogy), clear calendars for kickoff. (6) Coherence and messy middle - difficulty connecting low-level work to larger bets, need to keep reiterating bets in 1-3 quarter range, coherence â‰  certainty, manufacturing certainty limits coherence. (7) Multiple operating models - need different approaches for different types of bets, avoid one-size-fits-all process, higher-performing teams have stable patterns while absorbing unique work. (8) Data as trust proxy - data won't settle all disagreements, effective teams have more uncertainty, adopt learning stance, measurement is catalyst for learning not pass/fail. (9) Chronic vs acute issues - difference is self-repair ability, successful companies prevent chronic issues, immune system approach, variety in how achieved. (10) Short-term thinking - balancing act, be stubborn about long-term, compress activity durations, sense early indicators. (11) Time to get good - 12-18 months for full onboarding, requires repetition and practice, allow failure, psychological safety. (12) Play less Tetris - PMs loading up teams like Tetris, keeps people busy but prevents experimentation, over-constrains plans. (13) Moving fast and slow - bias for frequent integration + longer-term thinking, harmonize learning and shipping. (14) Storytelling - high-performing teams tell coherent stories with depth, discuss assumptions/learnings/surprises, practice reflection muscle. (15) Strategy and beliefs - "Which wave are you riding?", map core beliefs about industry direction, position relative to legacy/disruptors/competitors. (16) Prioritizing opportunities - value/effort framework is too simplistic, prioritize by opportunity size, resist premature breakdown of solutions. (17) Learning cadence - differentiate from shipping cadence, ask about Aha moments, learning velocity reveals team effectiveness, importance of acting on learning. (18) Learned helplessness - teams adapt to overly prescriptive PMs, engineers check out after being burned, need psychological safety. (19) Data snacking vs integrated - cherry-picking vs weaving data into all facets, consistent language around bets, common thread across reviews. (20) Functional feature factories - middle category (4-7 impact), chugging along but no 10s, lack step changes. (21) Teams closer to customers - "How long to get customer on phone?", remove proxies, connect problem solvers directly with humans, behavioral data + qualitative in tandem. (22) One pagers - flexible formats, checklists not requirements, don't force estimates for opportunity-focused bets. (23) Leaving time to iterate - teams scared without iteration time, frame missions with stopping functions, balance flexibility with confidence. (24) PMs creating environment - "best decisions happen reasonably quickly", not sole decider, create context for independent decisions, balance speed/quality. (25) Two priority levels - extremely valuable + experimentation-friendly OR reasonably valuable + guaranteed small, avoid medium-value trap. (26) Artificial deadlines - real deadline = value drops precipitously if missed, admit artificial deadlines, use cost of delay instead. (27) Forcing functions - healthy functions snap out of automatic thought (sprints, time boxes, customer exposure), definition from HCI glossary. (28) Measurement to inform decisions - measure what reduces uncertainty for decisions you need to make, not what competitors measure or for pass/fail. (29) Reflection is what counts - create projections at kickoff, revisit periodically, regular decision reviews, avoid revisionist history. (30) Shared understanding/vocabulary - always harder than you think, words like sprint/vision/MVP misunderstood, debate is what matters not single definition. (31) Products/features temporary - delivery mechanisms are temporary, knowledge of value exchange persists, teams around value streams not technology.

### Simple Product Management Tricks

- **Source:** Jacob Kaplan-Moss Blog
- **Author:** Jacob Kaplan-Moss
- **URL:** https://jacobian.org/2021/oct/20/simple-pm-tricks/
- **Date:** 2025
- **Key Concepts:** Product management, prioritization, effort/impact analysis, timeboxing, estimation, automation, playbooks, planning, backlog management
- **Key Insight:** Three simple, practical product management techniques for non-PMs who need to wear a product hat: (1) Effort/Impact Analysis - technique for long-term planning using 3x3 matrix mapping difficulty (horizontal) against impact (vertical). Fast and loose approach - don't worry about precise definitions. Upper-left (high impact, low effort) = obvious wins. Bottom-right (low impact, high effort) = obvious no's. Pick items from middle based on appetite. Remarkably effective at getting team agreement on work set quickly. Learned from Craig Kerstiens at Heroku. (2) Timebox Hard-to-Estimate Work - for work with huge unknowns (e.g., legacy migrations where unknowns dominate timeline, impossible to know upfront what tasks required). Solution: timeboxing - pick arbitrary length of time (week or two), see how far you get. At end, have more knowledge, review progress, make better-informed decisions. Powerful because approaches estimation differently - instead of "how long will X take?" asks "what can we do in two weeks?". Also useful for projects that can't be completed in reasonable time (e.g., massive bug backlog - untenable to drive to zero, but one day a week might be fine). Great for making progress despite mountains of work or significant unknowns. (3) Write Playbooks Before Automation - automation isn't always slam-dunk we think. We know automation isn't necessarily easy, but process is complex/error-prone/messy. Playbooks as middle ground - instead of doing nothing or diving into code, first write playbook (set of instructions/recipe). Be as specific as possible, include code/shell commands for copy-paste. Gets closer to automation goals (consistency, repeatability, reliability) at fraction of effort. If later decide automation worth it, playbook serves as detailed specification for code.

### Product-Driven versus Customer-Driven

- **Source:** Ribbonfarm
- **Author:** Venkatesh Rao
- **URL:** https://www.ribbonfarm.com/2014/04/24/product-driven-versus-customer-driven/
- **Date:** 2025
- **Key Concepts:** Product-driven businesses, customer-driven businesses, product strategy, business philosophy, Lean Startup critique, Steve Jobs, innovation, market creation, disruption, anomie, authoritah, True Auteur North, MVP, pivot, pattern recognition, new-game assumption
- **Key Insight:** Comprehensive framework distinguishing two fundamentally different approaches to building businesses. Product-driven businesses develop visions inside-out, create customers and wealth by selling structure into anomie (undefined restless energy), require an "indispensable asshole at the top" who is an instrument of "what technology wants," and expand markets absolutely. Customer-driven businesses develop visions outside-in through pattern recognition, steal rather than create customers (wealth redistribution, not creation), assume stable behaviors that don't change, and operate in zero-sum or negative-sum ways. Key distinctions: (1) Product-driven is NOT auteur-driven (singular vision within limited scope), itch-driven (personal convenience sampling), or formalism-driven (codified processes replacing asshole). (2) Cannot hybridize - attempts like Lean Startup create "visioning-process debt" where inside-out and outside-in drives eventually conflict. The pivot is insufficient to resolve this. (3) Product-driven sells structure (experience structure) while customer-driven sells happiness/solutions to existing patterns. (4) The "new-game assumption" - product-driven is better now because old economy is seizing up (stealing customers harder), new economy keeps growing. Question: boundless frontier (secular change) vs cyclic (50-70 year cycles, back to customer-driven in 30-50 years). Examples: Apple (P) vs Microsoft (C), Pixar (P) vs Dreamworks (C), Google (P) vs Samsung (C), Facebook (P) vs LinkedIn (C). Edge cases: Amazon and Elon Musk appear customer-driven but are product-driven (disruption is side-effect, TAN lies in new markets). Answers: build product-driven (easier to find money), cannot transform (DNA), product-driven is better (for now), identify by indispensable asshole at top aligned to TAN, suits winners/product-driven personalities, cannot hybridize (no-man's-land), don't listen to customers (they don't exist yet if product-driven).

### How to Develop Product Sense

- **Source:** Lenny's Newsletter
- **Author:** Jules Walter (Guest author; Product Leader at Google Gemini, previously at YouTube and Slack)
- **URL:** https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/product-sense
- **Date:** 2025
- **Key Concepts:** Product sense, PM skills, empathy, creativity, user research, product observation, product deconstruction, learning from product thinkers, technology trends, user behavior
- **Key Insight:** Comprehensive practical guide to developing product sense, which is the skill of consistently crafting products (or making changes) that have the intended impact on users. Product sense relies on empathy (discover meaningful user needs) and creativity (solutions that effectively address those needs). Four practices: (1) Observe people interacting with products - repeatedly observe people using products (yours or others), attend user research sessions, pay attention to micro details, ask open-ended questions. Key questions: "What do you think the purpose of this product is?", "What actions do you want to take?", "What are you thinking right now? How does that make you feel?". Five lessons: people are time-crunched/distracted (pick right defaults, make primary actions obvious), drop out when confused/nervous (unambiguous labels), don't give too much info at once (Slack pricing example - dropdowns increased purchases), context impacts decisions (comparisons, social proof), make goals/actions clear (Slack onboarding experiment increased retention). (2) Deconstruct everyday products - observe yourself using products (1-2 hours/month), use questions from Julie Zhuo's product critique framework, compare products in same category (Cash App vs Venmo example - different strategies), observe own reactions (frustration and delight moments), meditation helps improve observation skills. (3) Learn from great product thinkers - attend product reviews, take notes, look for patterns in questions/feedback, understand mental checklists and principles. Example: Stewart Butterfield at Slack - "The details are not the details. They make the design." (rooster @channel warning example), understand problems and frame them with strong constraints (streamlines decision-making). If don't work at company with great thinkers: find external mentors, read articles/interviews (Julie Zhuo, Stewart Butterfield, David Lieb, Rahul Vohra), ask questions about prompts, decision points, alternative approaches, surprising insights, principles/frameworks. (4) Be curious about technology and domain - learn about emerging trends (technology, society, regulations). Examples: Cash App + Bitcoin (76% revenue), Uber (smartphones/GPS/Google Maps APIs), Clubhouse (pandemic). Track macro trends (platform shifts - web3/AR/VR/AI, social shifts - remote work, regulations - privacy laws) and micro changes (Figma + WebGL example). Keep open mind, ask "If this trend reaches full potential, what opportunities will it create?" Keep up via developer conferences, industry analysts (Ben Thompson), Twitter (Naval, Elad Gil, Balaji), invest/advise startups or follow top VCs. Meet engineers/domain experts for micro changes. As Paul Graham said: "Live in the future, then build what's missing." Signs of improvement: notice subtle things (micro frustrations/delights), anticipate non-obvious problems, develop higher-quality hypotheses, contribute unique insights, be right more often about impact on metrics, get positive feedback. Additional practice: actively reflect on product sense loop (identify assumptions, construct experiments, analyze outcomes, reflect on right/wrong, repeat). If assumptions proven more frequently (e.g., 2/10 to 8/10), product sense improving. Resources: Tony Fadell TED talk, Marty Cagan's "Inspired", Don Norman course.

### Speed Generates Quality

- **Source:** Medium (Jason Yip)
- **Author:** Jason Yip (Senior Manager Product Engineering at Grainger; ex-Spotify, ex-ThoughtWorks, ex-CruiseControl)
- **URL:** https://jchyip.medium.com/guiding-principle-speed-generates-quality-f0672db3e4bc
- **Date:** 2025
- **Key Concepts:** Product development, speed, quality, learning loops, iteration, organizational learning, velocity, agility
- **Key Insight:** Counterintuitive guiding principle: speed in learning generates quality, not the other way around. The ceramics teacher story: teacher divided class into two groups - "quantity" group graded on weight of pots produced (50 lbs = A), "quality" group graded on single perfect pot. Result: highest quality work came from quantity group. Why? Quantity group was churning out work and learning from mistakes, while quality group sat theorizing about perfection and had little to show. Core principle: Speed creates more opportunities for learning; learning generates quality. Mistake is believing that being more careful, taking longer, creates quality - sub-optimal because certain knowledge can only be acquired by releasing and making contact with the problem. This isn't mindless speed ("move fast and break things") but rather shortening learning loops. Learning faster is not about rushing; it's about making things safer and smoother. Key distinction: Smooth from perspective of "baton" (the work) â‰  smooth from perspective of "runner" (the worker). Smooth from perspective of baton has more impact on organizational learning speed. Two approaches: (1) Quality first - "be very careful," "do it right the first time," "people need to love first version" - leads to theorizing, little output, misses learning. (2) Speed first - "shorten learning loop," "make sure failure doesn't kill us" - produces iterations, learns from mistakes, generates better quality. Implications: Don't optimize for perfect first version, optimize for fast learning cycles. Create safety so failures don't kill you. Focus on smooth flow of work through system, not just individual speed. Fastest path to quality is through many cycles of learning, not careful planning of single attempt. Referenced: Art & Fear: Observations On the Perils (and Rewards) of Artmaking by David Bayles and Ted Orland (via Stratechery).

### Musk's 5-Step Design Process

- **Source:** ModelThinkers
- **Author:** Elon Musk (via interview with Everyday Astronaut)
- **URL:** https://modelthinkers.com/mental-model/musks-5-step-design-process
- **Date:** Unknown (interview date)
- **Key Concepts:** Design process, engineering efficiency, requirements, simplification, optimization, cycle time, automation, first principles, lean thinking
- **Key Insight:** Systematic 5-step design process that SpaceX aims to "apply rigorously" for efficient engineering and product development. The 5 steps (must be done in order): (1) Make the requirements less dumb - "your requirements are definitely dumb, it does not matter who gave them to you." Question the question. Requirements from "smart person" often most dangerous because you might not question them enough. Similar to Framestorming. (2) Delete the part or process - "If you're not adding things back in at least 10% of the time, you're clearly not deleting enough." Start lean, build up when required. Bias is to add things "in case" but you can make "in case" arguments for many things. Each requirement/constraint must be accountable to person, not department. Example: SpaceX grid fins didn't need to fold back - eliminated need for massive mechanisms. (3) Simplify or optimize the design - "Possibly the most common error of a smart engineer is to optimize a thing that should not exist." Must work through first two steps before optimizing. Engineers need holistic view. Example: Engineers invested in reducing rocket engine weight but hadn't addressed reducing payload weight equivalently. (4) Accelerate cycle time - "If you're digging your grave, don't dig faster." Embrace speed but only after steps 1-3 ensure right direction. Accelerated Agile approach, but only after first three steps satisfied. (5) Automate - Don't automate before earlier points addressed. Tesla Model 3 battery mat story: invested massive time/effort to automate process before asking what mat was for - discovered it was no longer required (created to reduce sound but unnecessary). Order is critical - must work through steps 1-3 before accelerating/automating. Can be applied to UX, marketing campaigns, product design. Reflects First Principle Thinking, Framestorming, Occam's Razor, Lean Startup/MVP (Musk refers to "Minimum Viable Rocket"). Rejects adding features "in case." Limitations: May need to cycle back and forth in complex applications as requirements are uncovered or refined.

### As a User, I Don't Want To (Inverted User Stories)

- **Source:** UX Collective (Medium)
- **Author:** Pavel Samsonov
- **URL:** https://uxdesign.cc/as-a-user-i-dont-want-to-973990a30158
- **Date:** 2025
- **Key Concepts:** User stories, product development, user experience, value vs cost, product complexity, feature bloat, product management, agile, scrum, innovation
- **Key Insight:** Task-oriented user stories lead to bad product decisions because they mix up value with cost. Original user story format: "As [user] I want [to reach a goal] so that I can [obtain a benefit]" - focused on goal and benefit. Degenerated into task-oriented: "As [user] I want [to do a task] so that I can [use the tool]" - focuses on work. Examples of corrupted stories: "I want to click the button so that I can see the page," "I want to enter my email so that I can receive exclusive offers," "As a user, I want to log in" (missing "so that"). The truth: users don't want to use a product at all - ideal experience is reaching goal without doing any work. Original format outlined benefit users might pay for, team figures out how to provide benefit and costs. But tasks, tools, and features are NOT benefits - they are costs users pay to achieve goal. Adding features degrades experience by increasing cost. With physical products, users have way out when complexity exceeds value. With software, they just leave. Task-oriented stories obfuscate lack of value - "as a user, I want to fill out the form" fools team into thinking user will give product something for nothing. Cost is hidden because user is defined as someone who wants to fill out forms, even if no such person exists. With stories like that, every ticket becomes self-justifying. Features multiply, complexity rises, value plummets, but teams can't see it. Solution: Invert the user story. Task-oriented story contains useful information: what it calls benefit is actually the cost, what it calls goal is solution hypothesis. Example: "As a user, I want to customize my toolbar so that I can optimize my browsing experience" becomes "As a user, I want to optimize my browsing experience without having to customize my toolbar." This shifts focus from task to desired outcome, highlights feature as hypothesis needing validation, encourages innovation, reduces complexity. Working backwards: Task-oriented stories capture existing patterns that are obvious - if benefit brings obvious feature to mind, it probably exists elsewhere as cost. Existing cost is great place to find pain points. Amazon approach: "today, customers have to..." - "Today, customers have to customize the toolbar if they want to improve their browsing experience" sounds like struggle, not feature to build. Inverting stories turns rush to add ticket into opportunity to innovate and differentiate. Key takeaways: Original focused on goals/benefits, task-oriented focuses on work/tools, tasks/features are costs not benefits, invert by changing "I want to do X" to "I want Y without having to do X," reveals real goal and highlights assumptions, opens better solution space.

### Startups Don't Need Product Managers Who Are Visionaries (Street Sweeper PM)

- **Source:** VentureBeat
- **Author:** Jon Sadow (Cofounder and Chief Product Officer at Scoop; previously at Google)
- **URL:** https://venturebeat.com/entrepreneur/startups-dont-need-product-managers-who-are-visionaries
- **Date:** 2025
- **Key Concepts:** Product management, startup PM, visionary vs facilitator, street sweeper, prioritization, MVP, embracing what you don't know, team relationships, hiring product managers
- **Key Insight:** Counterintuitive perspective: startups don't need product managers striving to be visionaries. Maximum benefit comes from someone who focuses less on glamour/invention and more on empowering teams to build most impactful product possible. Core of great PM: clearing path for everyone else on team to set them up for success. Four key practices: (1) Be a street sweeper - focus on mundane but critical tasks. Example: International product rollout at Google required focusing on finance law, translation services, font size details for different languages rather than exciting international travel. Without mundane conversations about font size or finance law, product never would have launched. When managers dream up next great product, they often aren't addressing mundane but critical tasks. Refusal to sweep street (or belief they're above it) leads to ineffective processes, delayed development/deployment. (2) Prioritize, prioritize, prioritize - example of feature development where team initially wanted full vision with bells and whistles. Realized needed street sweeper mentality: clear road of complicated capabilities, prioritize simplest version. Visionary approach would never have launched - would prioritize idea rather than MVP. Visionary PMs drawn to revolutionary/idyllic rather than evolutionary. Must be comfortable asking what's most important, prioritizing necessary less-flashy capabilities, accepting iterative rollout/slimmer product. (3) Embrace what you don't know - PMs come from technical or non-technical backgrounds, can polarize dissonance. Example: Technical PM writing code instead of facilitating slowed development, poor resource use, suggests air of knowing better. Visionary PM might feel they have all answers. How well manager fills knowledge gaps matters more. Must surround with experts in designing, building, marketing, distributing. Embrace their knowledge, don't make decisions beyond expertise, avoid leaning too heavily on familiar. (4) Build mutually respectful relationships - mentor advice: PMs should [almost] always let everyone else take credit while they take blame. Not just to make people feel good - when PM expends energy showing visionary greatness, damages team morale/motivation. Visionary PMs who don't sweep streets get in way of team efforts. Managers dreaming up idyllic vision slow development, silence other voices. Shows process is about catering to PM's whims/ego rather than building right product. Must give teammates respect, show appreciation, make clear success tied to team success. In practice, team actually deserves credit (PM didn't write code, make designs, create marketing). Once team understands PM support, they return trust/support. Visionary PMs influence development cycle in ways detrimental to process. Startups need someone who: appreciates street sweeper role, prioritizes well, embraces what they don't know, invests in relationships. Building great products is about iterating and supporting smooth development process. For leadership hiring PM: ask what candidate thinks their job will be. Do they see themselves sitting in back room thinking of next big thing? Or as facilitator? Answer is telling - right approach engenders sustained success.

### GitLab Product Principles

- **Source:** GitLab Handbook
- **Author:** GitLab (Company-wide product principles)
- **URL:** https://handbook.gitlab.com/handbook/product/product-principles/
- **Date:** 2025)
- **Key Concepts:** Product principles, customer-centric innovation, customer zero, design-led, quality over velocity, data-driven, iteration, MVC (Minimal Valuable Change), convention over configuration, subtractive thinking, integration principles, product-led growth, feature naming, next-generation development, user experience, ambition, product usage, flow one, small primitives, feature ownership
- **Key Insight:** Comprehensive set of product principles for building world-class products through customer-centric innovation. Eight core principles: (1) We are customer zero - use our own product, challenge "why" if feature isn't something Engineering would use. (2) We are not our only customer - meet with customers, validate assumptions through user research/interviews/feedback. (3) We are design-led - UX is top priority, technical product should be easy for someone new to get started. (4) We value quality over velocity - everything must be user validated, bug free, secure, scalable, documented, available on all deployments. No technical debt in favor of velocity. (5) We value data over intuition - measure outcomes not launches, all features instrumented for success metrics. (6) We fail fast and iterate with intention - define hypothesis, validate through problem validation, build iteration strategy, revalidate through solution validation. (7) Product-led growth - product should be best Sales team member, enable feature discovery moments. (8) We win as a team - highest execution and collaboration across Product and cross-functional teams. Key frameworks: Minimal Valuable Change (MVC) - smallest measurable improvement with four pillars: relentless customer focus, measurable outcomes, product functionality adhering to GA criteria, future vision. MVC means reducing scope to ship quickly, not shipping something that hurts usability. First impressions important. Examples: ship via API before UI, expose minimal functionality. Not advised for core experiences. Iteration - break problems down small, focus on reduced cycle time. Each iteration must define expected impact and establish metrics for success. Success measured by achieving objectives, not deployment. Subtractive thinking - humans favor adding features, PMs should recognize bias and remove features when more efficient. Convention over Configuration - reduce complexity while offering needed features. Working by default (enabled + setup by default). Avoid limits, avoid configuration when possible. Configuration principles: builds up over time, hard to remove, expensive testing mechanism (use feature flags instead). Avoid "Not Invented Here" - leverage existing technology to get to market quickly, contribute to Open Source, focus on making GitLab better. Integrate thoughtfully - don't clone everything, prevent broken workflows for free, charge for adding value, respect security/permissions, use empty states for discovery, use own design language, be transparent about data source. Avoid plugins/marketplaces - open core allows adding directly to codebase, works out of box, tested together, no upgrade fears. Product naming - descriptive not distinctive names (efficient, inclusive), use prepositions for third-party integrations. Next Generation: Modern first (modern teams before legacy), Developer first (start with developer then other personas), Cloud-native first (then support rest), Prioritize current adopters (optimize for current/future adopters over early adopters). UX principles: Strive for simplicity, Depth over breadth, Better than before (MVC not better than nothing), Timeless design. Be Ambitious - accomplish "impossible" things, velocity unchanged whether scheduled ambitiously or with slack. Drive Product Usage - AARRR framework (single feature), Customer Adoption Journey (cross-adoption). Golden Journey: Create â†’ Plan â†’ Verify â†’ Release. Flow One - workflow of MVCs for specific narrow use-case, avoid inflexible long-term plan. Additional: Avoid enforced workflows (allow enterprise flexibility at group level, default off), Prefer small primitives (building blocks, can be combined), Every feature owned by group (unowned = unsupervised, accrues debt), Always allow deploying to production (safeguards removable in emergency), Data-driven work (tools for both SaaS/self-managed, start from question).

### How to Hire a Product Manager

- **Source:** Bring the Donuts (Ken Norton's Blog)
- **Author:** Ken Norton (Executive Coach, Former Google Product Leader)
- **URL:** https://www.bringthedonuts.com/essays/productmanager.html
- **Date:** 2025, updated 2023
- **Key Concepts:** Product management, hiring product managers, PM skills, product instincts, technical background, leadership, shipping products, startup hiring, product management career
- **Key Insight:** Classic essay that defined the product manager role and launched thousands of PM careers. Reality: Product management may be the one job organization would get along fine without (at least for a while). Without engineers, nothing built; without sales, nothing sold; without designers, product looks like crap. But without PMs, everyone fills in gap and goes on. Important reminder: as PM, you're expendable. In long run great product management makes difference between winning/losing, but you have to prove it. Product management is weird discipline full of oddballs and rejects that never quite fit in anywhere else - combines elements of engineering, design, marketing, sales, business development. Six key characteristics: (1) Hire all the smart people - most importantly, raw intellectual horsepower. Take wickedly smart inexperienced PM over average intellect with years of experience. Product management is about thinking on feet, staying ahead of competitors, projecting into minds of colleagues/customers. Ask analytical questions until sure candidate is smarter than you. Best candidates bounce out of chairs with excitement when posed theoretical problems. Update 2023: Don't ask brainteaser/logic puzzle questions - move toward structured interviews with work samples and situational problems without "right answer." (2) Strong technical background - not necessarily CS degree, but favor people who've been in technical roles. Gives ability to relate to engineers and grasp technical details. Technical PMs more successful conveying requirements to engineers and relaying details to non-technical colleagues/customers. Critical pitfall: PM who's former engineer needs to realize they're FORMER engineer. PMs who still try to take charge of technical decisions/implementation details will crash spectacularly. Prefer technical people who've already made move to product management at previous job - they've gone through adaptation period. Interview questions: Why move from engineering to PM? Biggest advantage/disadvantage of technical background? Biggest lesson learned? What wish you'd known as engineer? How earn respect of engineering team? (3) "Spidey-sense" product instincts and creativity - highly subjective, difficult to evaluate, extraordinarily important. Certain people born with innate product instincts - just know what makes great product, instincts usually point right direction, passionate advocates. Small decisions where great PM distances from decent one. Know they've got spidey-sense when suggest approaches nobody thought of but immediately strike everyone as obvious. Evaluating in interview: check if candidate (a) independently echoed your concerns about product, (b) taught you something new about product (shows not afraid to speak critically, probably smarter), (c) turned you on to something new and interesting (people with great instincts notice great products before everyone else). Interview questions: Tell me about great product you've encountered recently (drives crazy when candidates name your products). What made [iPod/eBay] successful? What do you dislike about my product? How improve it? What problems will we encounter in 1/2/10 years? How know product well designed? Best/worst ideas ever had? How know when to cut corners? Lessons about UI design? How decide what not to build? Biggest product mistake? Least interesting aspects of PM? Consider yourself creative? Update 2023: Product designers ascended to "third leg of stool" - include product designers in interview loop. (4) Leadership that's earned - PMs usually leaders but don't have direct line authority, earn authority and lead by influence. Leadership and interpersonal skills critical. Reference checks most effective way to measure, especially peers/individual contributors who worked with but didn't report to candidate. Interview questions: Is consensus always good? Difference between management and leadership? Kinds of people like/difficult to work with? Time team didn't gel? How get team to commit to schedule? What lose your confidence? Manage people from different functions differently? Learned about saying no? Ultimate accountability for shipping? Team let you down and you took blame? Tolerance for mistakes changed? Good news or bad news first? Approach to hiring? (5) Ability to channel multiple points-of-view - requires wearing multiple hats, advocate for whoever isn't in room (customer, engineering, sales, executives, marketing). Capable of doing other people's jobs but smart enough to know not to. Play devil's advocate, unsatisfied with simple answers. Evaluating: get lots of people in interview process. At minimum, representatives from engineering, design, marketing. Can grow to pre-sales engineering, support, developer relations, business development, legal, customers. One carefully selected representative of each key function. Nobody judges understanding of sales process like salesperson. Give specific instructions to interviewers. Interview questions: How learned to work with sales? Best way to interface with customers? What makes marketing tick? How know when design on right track? How PM support business development? Learned about managing up? Best way to work with executives? (6) Give me someone who's shipped something - unless position very junior, hire PMs who've actually shipped product (start to finish, concept to launch). Nothing better indication of ability to ship great products than having done it before. Past performance indicates future success. Check references with manager and engineering/sales/marketing counterparts. Note: Rules ordered for reason - take brilliantly smart PM over dimmer experienced one even if former hasn't shipped. Startup vs Big Company: Startup needs strong generalists who adapt (everyone does little of everything, hard to predict future). Big company has specific roles (lower likelihood of change). Deadly thinking at startup: "seems suited for this very specific role" - works at big company, deadly at startup.

### How to Work Faster in a Remote Team

- **Date:** 2025
- **Key Concepts:** Remote work, productivity, distributed teams, communication tools, remote team practices, time management, work environment, asynchronous work, meeting efficiency
- **Key Insight:** Practical guide based on real experiences from remote-first companies. Fundamental difference: Non-remote work defaults to highest distraction communication (in-person), remote work defaults to lowest (no communication). Fewer distractions lead to faster work - you have to purposely decide to enter more distracting conversations. Remote work succeeds in environments with little red tape and low barrier to decision making. Eight ways to work faster: (1) Pull internal communication out of inbox - only two types of internal emails at Zapier (interview feedback, new hire announcements). Move updates/specs/mock-ups to internal blog, feedback/discussions to Slack. Communication feels cheap but adds up to lots of time costs. Streamlining and cutting back-and-forth is true benefit. (2) Rely on right tools - when team lands on right tools, get in groove. Master communication tools (Google Docs, Trello, Slack, Flowdock, video chat). Twilio example: more efficient than in-person because mastery over tools, writing-review-revision feedback loop inherently asynchronous. If one tool doesn't work, move to next. (3) Use differing schedules to advantage - flexible hours for peak productivity (overcome natural lulls with naps/workouts), work when most productive, 24/7 development with time zones (customers get help any hour, production never stops), handoff advantage (end of day in one timezone = start of next day marked up in another). (4) Skip commute - save dozens of hours weekly, zero friction between working and not, apply same principle (prepare lunch, use thermos, cut excess processes). (5) Cancel meetings - video conference annoyance helps you avoid them. Ask: do we really need that meeting? Earn back dozens of hours monthly (one org identified 7,000 hours saved). Example: iDoneThis reconfigured unfocused weekly meetings to show-and-tell sessions. (6) Think before you speak (send) - written communication forces thought, extra steps add filter, clearer/more concise communication. Amazon approach: ban PowerPoint, require memos (full sentences harder to write, force clear thinking). Chat is more to the point, skips chit-chat. (7) Set up best environment - Jason Fried insight: people rarely say "the office" when asked where they get things done. Office shreds day into work moments (15 min here, 30 min there). Remote: control environment (quiet home), change surroundings quickly (stand/sit/couch), work from anywhere (mall/coffee shop/park bench). First telecommuting experience: finishing tasks much earlier, working better in environment. (8) Own up to productivity - responsible for productivity, must own what you did/didn't accomplish. Example: Belle Cooper cut blog post time from 2 days to 4 hours by examining what held her up (cut excess research, distractions). Practical tip: Trello board for to-dos, automation for weekly recaps (cut from 40 to 20 min). Downsides: time zone delays (waiting for responses from people asleep), lone remote member challenges (harder to communicate via whiteboards), infrastructure issues (shoddy internet). Key insight: might not need to go fully remote - instant messaging apps, collaborative editing, file sharing already shift how we communicate. Even companies that don't allow remote work encourage it implicitly through equipment and habits. Personal tip: completely unplug from communication tools, put 100% focus in distraction-free environment.

Effective distributed team is effective team - evaluate same way. What makes distributed different is intention and advanced planning - doesn't happen by accident, requires deliberate systems around communication, coordination, collaboration, organization, operations, culture. Six key areas: (1) Communication - default state is miscommunication. Assume someone missed message, repeat yourself, drop passive-aggressive tone. Neutral tones read negative, positive as neutral - overcorrect with emoji/GIFs/explicit communication. Skip sarcasm/irony. Synchronous vs asynchronous: synchronous for real-time exchange, bonds teams, vital for creative ideation, easier to spot problems. Don't let be ad hoc - get teams on cadence. Need at least 2 hours overlap during regular working time. Hybrid needs active management to prevent co-located teams dominating scheduling. (3) Collaboration - not better than co-located, just different. Physically constrained means strategic changes need higher value threshold - many OSS projects successful with this. Glue work (whatever makes org successful - strategy, meetings, PM, design review, mentoring, coaching) - reward explicitly in promotion processes. Important for reducing gender wage gap (women carry burden in collaborative cultures). Face-to-face still critical - meet every 6 months, more than 2 days overwhelming. Plan like mini-conferences. Budget as recurring line item. As team grows, may yield diminishing returns. Collaboration is art and science - observe what works, adjust. (4) Organization - Brooks's law hits earlier. Keep teams small (3-4 engineers to 1 PM works well, 6-7+ becomes difficult). Cross-functional teams own customer-facing outcome, reduce interdependencies, decisions made close to problem. Resist adding every function - embed by having representatives. Working groups organized by function (informal discussion or formalized with specific task) counteract loss of domain expertise/standardization. In orgs 30+ people, delivery team (SREs, FREs, QREs) can own "delivering software" - clear roadblocks, support getting work out door. Embrace overlap between roles - encourage constructive feedback across teams. (5) Operations - bridge strategy with execution. Equipment: minimum is laptop, headphones, decent internet. Buffer reimburses basics plus fast home internet/coworking, new laptops on join, replace after 3 years. Splice offers home-office improvement stipend (peripherals, plants), ships monitor/laptop preference, office-chair stipend. Hiring remotely: mirror how you'll work (video calls not in-person). Pay attention to clarity of written communication, responsiveness, punctuality, communication about delays. Ask about independent work, initiative, growth mindset, unblocking themselves. Better predictors than "worked remotely before." Technical exercises with clear rubrics, evaluate mindful communication too. Onboarding: detailed 30/60/90 day plans, check in at each mark, give buddies. Without this, new people band together leading to misinformation/culture fragmentation. Distributed decision-making: adopt RFC process (Rust/Ember style) - space for proposals, not seeking consensus. (6) Culture - don't try to fulfill utopian expectations. Experiences will be different - that's okay. Not everyone thrives remotely - extroverts can feel lonely/isolated, anxiety/depression more common, flexibility can mean loss of structure, freedom can feel like abandonment. Traffic light check-ins (red/yellow/green) at start/end of meetings - quick way to see how people doing, helps humans support each other. Reinforce culture constantly or become network of freelancers. Stated values essential - can't rely on office vibe. Hybrid: distributed portion lives company like TV show, leaders should work remotely to build empathy. Global company culture can transcend local norms. Lavish public praise for values-aligned behavior, swift/firm discouraging what's not. Work-life boundaries: remote workers work longer (sometimes full extra day/week). Manager concern isn't if working but if they ever stop. Model healthy boundaries, turn off chat, take vacation offline, don't develop "always-on" norm. Benefits: distribute opportunity globally, cultural diversity enriches, autonomy/flexibility, scale faster/cheaper, pop geographic bubbles. Downsides: less IRL time, tendency to hermit (schedule out-of-home activities), FOMO. Final: be explicit about constraints and trade-offs, decisions are yours to make, org will evolve.

### Scaling Engineering Teams via RFCs: Writing Things Down

- **Source:** The Pragmatic Engineer Blog
- **Author:** Gergely Orosz (Author of The Software Engineer's Guidebook; previously at Uber, Microsoft, Skype, Skyscanner)
- **URL:** https://blog.pragmaticengineer.com/scaling-engineering-teams-via-writing-things-down-rfcs/
- **Date:** 2025, last updated September 21, 2022
- **Key Concepts:** RFCs, Request for Comments, scaling engineering teams, documentation, engineering planning, architecture, knowledge sharing, transparency, engineering culture, design docs, ADRs
- **Key Insight:** Simple five-step RFC process that scales engineering teams from tens to thousands of engineers, successfully used at Uber and other large tech companies. Addresses two common planning issues: (1) lack of visibility on others building same thing, (2) tech/architecture debt from different teams building differently. Five steps: (1) Do planning before building - whiteboarding or talking through with team, be clear on how to get things done. (2) Capture plan in short written document - once clear to team, should be quick to write down "how", don't go overboard. (3) Have select people approve before starting - similar to PR reviews, have relevant people (senior engineers, users) validate planned work. (4) Send planning document to all engineers in company and let anyone comment - yes, sounds crazy but works. (5) Have everyone follow steps for every project beyond trivial complexity, iterate on lightweight RFC-like process. Process works and scales well, addresses visibility, reduces tech/architecture debt, spreads knowledge, engages engineers. Power of writing things down: creates accountability, leads to more thorough decisions. Examples: code review in writing increases quality, written agenda makes meetings less wasteful, writing down plans reduces surprises. Efficiency argument flipped: if everyone agrees, writing should be easy (few hours). Reality: usually less straightforward - reveals issues: "This is not what I meant", "What about this edge case?", "If we change this, could break that part" - great to have these discussions before project halfway done. Reviewers and spreading knowledge: writing for others different from organizing thoughts, specify who needs to read/give thumbs-up, require confirmation in writing. Sending to everyone: sounds crazy, creates noise (manageable with email filters), less plans sent than expected even with hundreds/thousands engineers. Cultural impact: type of information pushed shapes culture - pushing plans to everyone and inviting comments sets tone of trust/responsibility. Personal experience: reading planning docs from variety of teams learned more about other teams' work in months than years at large company without visibility. Self-balancing/correction: allowing anyone to chime in keeps consistent engineering bar - teams on one side realize other side doing similar with different approach/quality bar. Example: US team building feature without considering other parts of world, India team points out localization gaps. Transparency enables self-balancing/correction naturally. Tailoring via iteration: process refined as company grew - started as email to every engineer, changed to mailing lists per domain (backend, mobile, web), templates built by engineers, more tooling for thousands of engineers. Template evolution: reviewers had same questions ("What is motivation?", "How will this be tested?", "Will architecture changes be made?"), engineers created templates that keep being updated. Backend template: approvers, abstract, architecture changes, service SLAs, service dependencies, load/performance testing, multi data-center concerns, security, testing/rollout, metrics/monitoring, customer support. Mobile/Web template: approvers, abstract, UI/UX, architecture changes, network interactions, library dependencies, security, testing/rollout, analytics, customer support, accessibility. Key: iterate/customize to team needs - templates include what they care about (reliability, scale, security). At Uber: many smaller services so load/performance testing/SLAs important. When accessibility became focus for mobile, section added. Process scales: called RFC (Request for Comments) - similarities with RFC publication process in tech community. Stuck because started early (when team small), helped scale knowledge/eliminate silos, scaled from tens to low thousands. New challenges at few thousand engineers (good problem). Real-world example: BaseUI open source project shows RFC process - all RFCs published/reviewed publicly, development only starts after RFC approved. Not unique to Uber: Google (design docs), Facebook, Microsoft, Amazon all have similar RFC processes. Architecture Decision Records (ADRs) also good format. Scale/thoroughness/who docs pushed to/strictness/standardization differs by company. Key takeaway: if planning still adhoc, having RFC process in place early on helps engineering scale greatly. Just try and iterate - that's how it always starts.

### Scaling Engineering Organizations

- **Source:** Stripe Atlas Guides
- **Author:** Raylene Yung (Led efforts to expand Stripe globally, bringing product and engineering teams to new markets; previously engineering director at Facebook; BS and MS in computer science from Stanford)
- **URL:** https://stripe.com/in/guides/atlas/scaling-eng

- **Key Concepts:** Scaling engineering teams, recruiting, hiring, training, onboarding, engagement, retention, candidate experience, rubrics, bias training, psychological safety, employee feedback, hackathons, rotations
- **Key Insight:** Lessons learned from scaling Stripe's engineering team. Core philosophy: deliberate choice to add engineers at slower rate than user base growth, take iterative approach to recruiting and developing team. Keep teams small, invest more in each person, nimbly change ways of working. Built culture of feedback and iterative thinking - what works for 50 engineers may not work for 10 or 1000. Three main areas: (1) Recruiting and Hiring - Continuously evolve process (run well for 6-9 months, then update). Build consistent recruiting platform - increases efficiency, prevents bias, raises hiring bar. Run regular training sessions for new hiring managers, clear documentation for small details. Candidate Review process: recurring meeting with experienced reviewers to examine all interview results, give feedback to interviewers, identify improvements. Each detail affects recruiting process: job descriptions, recruiter staffing, specialized interview questions/rubrics/prep guides. Create great candidate experience - interview defines first impression. Benchmark: 12-20% acceptance rate from onsite to offer. For every hire, 4-7 people go elsewhere - they represent your company. Likely to hire from candidates' networks - great impression generates referrals. Best practices: written guides before interview, engineers choose language/use own laptop, follow-up anonymous surveys, speak with every candidate, calls for those who don't accept, personalized feedback when no offer. Results: without offers rate 4.1/5, with offers 4.5/5. Candidates who don't join often refer others. (2) Training and Onboarding - Early mistake: not investing enough, sink-or-swim experience, teammates thought it was "test" to evaluate again. Today invest significant time/energy. First months critical - tempting to ask engineer to build feature first week, but investing in onboarding makes happier/more productive. Stripe 101: general onboarding for all employees, connects new hires across company. Few weeks: classes, meet cofounders, build Stripe integration, answer support tickets, collaborate across functions. Scale: 30 sessions, 82 instructors, 200+ employees last year. Results: 100% summarize 3+ Stripe products, 94.5% positive first week/effective use of time, 90% understand Stripe stack. New hires graduate with immediate network across functions - helps navigate organization. /dev/start: engineering-specific onboarding. New engineers anxious to spend time with technical teams, dive into code. Solve practical problem in main application codebase. Small pilot: handful engineers, volunteer mentors, projects from backlogs, one-on-one feedback. Expanded to all engineering. Today: group into temporary virtual teams for 3-4 weeks, dedicated mentor, learn development process while shipping concrete tool/product improvement. Mentors gain project leadership/mentorship experience - sit with team, break down tasks, answer technical/product questions, review code, bridge to rest of engineering. Don't forget soft skills/culture - psychological safety (Google Project Aristotle: most important factor). Write guides to culture/group norms - general communication and team-specific. "Communicating at Stripe" class: mailing lists, email/bug tracker best practices, non-obvious patterns like scheduling meetings. Calendar norms carry cultural knowledge. Example: Email at Stripe - non-urgent use email, quick response use Slack, OOO autoresponder, send shipped@ email, don't lurk unnecessarily, safe environment for open communication. Engineering teams provide specialized guides for tools (GitHub, JIRA) or practices (code review) - source of truth, collaborative agreement, reduce debate/confusion. Self-serve documentation decreases reliance on oral tradition, scales faster. (3) Engagement and Retention - Invest in team you have. Employee happiness: know impact of work, feel autonomy in decisions. Successful teams: provide structure for long-term engagement, actively listen to feedback, define clear paths for growth, train leaders. Companies can focus short-term output OR long-term productivity. High-growth: conditions change rapidly, early decisions have lasting impact, early employees carry disproportionate knowledge, most effective, greatest mentors, but can feel overloaded/trapped. Collaborate with early employees to build right supporting structure. Don't mistake productivity for engagement - "surprise resignation" story: long-tenured engineer consistently delivering high-quality work, challenging projects, mentoring, quietly resigned at one-on-one. No longer felt challenged - couldn't see way to increase impact and learn new skills. Stuck in local maximum - easier to leave for exciting opportunity than less-impactful internal role. Realization: simple conversation about career aspirations would have revealed concern. People stay engaged when feel personal sense of growth - look beyond day-to-day productivity, ask right questions. StripeSat survey twice yearly: opportunities to learn/grow, see yourself at Stripe in 3 years, good career opportunities (stretch assignments, responsibility, internal mobility). Create supported paths for mobility/learning - help people discover opportunities, create independent projects, shape story/role. Internal resources: advertise new teams, encourage rotations, run hackathons. Rotations: learn new language, explore different stack, sometimes permanently switch teams. Past 6 months: dozen+ engineers rotated between iOS mobile, core payment API, ML. Highlight roles for internal transfers - bootstrapping new team, complex technical problem. Direct longest-tenured engineers to projects where provide most help. Hackathons: don't need weeks of planning or new team. Recent hackathon: 80 participants across offices, shipped 7 projects to internal/external users (right-to-left language support, internal productivity tools). Ask for feedback and respond - requires scalable approach and careful listening. Employees recognize problems but don't always know how to translate to effective change. Three steps: identify problem, design way to collect structured feedback, address feedback/deliver results. Developer Experience survey example: engineers described dev environment as slow/inefficient. Formed team of experienced engineers focused on internal developer experience. Intuition/expertise not enough to identify most impactful projects. Launched survey: hours lost due to dev process problems, 1-2 things to make more productive, what's harder than it should be. Results: most common request (by factor of 2!) was faster/more reliable edit-sync-test loop. Team tackled projects to improve speed/reliability of syncing/reloading/testing. Next round months later: satisfaction improved 50-80%. Created positive feedback loop, engineers felt heard. Now run Developer Experience surveys every quarter. Key principle: how you scale team has enormous impact on culture, productivity, employee engagement, business success. At quickly growing companies, conditions change rapidly - what works today may not hold tomorrow. Continue iterating, develop new practices as grow.

### Managing Your Time as a Middle Manager

- **Source:** The Heartbeat Newsletter (Canopy)
- **Author:** Claire Lew (Founder & CEO of Canopy)
- **URL:** https://newsletter.canopy.is/p/managing-your-time-as-a-middle-manager
- **Date:** 2025
- **Key Concepts:** Middle management, time management, player-coach, leadership, prioritization, fluid focus, context switching, work-life balance, management challenges
- **Key Insight:** How to allocate time when caught in the middle between direct reports and supervisors - the "player-coach" tension. Core problem: Leaders feel squeezed at both ends, overwhelmed, never knowing if priority is right, depleted trying to keep up. The toothpaste tube analogy: squeezed from both ends, no one concerned middle won't fit. Player-coach tension: On one end, expected to map team goals, hold 1:1s (coach role). On other end, asked to deliver tactical work - writing code, designing feature, creating marketing campaign (player role). Not inherently bad - player work keeps you connected to passion, keeps skills sharp. But lived experience feels bad - overwhelmed, depleted. DO NOT: Chase balance or believe perfect distribution exists. Balance is illusion. No magical formula. What's "best" depends on context - what's required of you, team, organization in that moment. DO: Figure out what's most high-leverage for your specific context. Ask: What's most urgent? What is team success most contingent on? What's most critical to team/organization at this moment? Might discover need to step back and nurture team, or double down on personal deliverables first. Examine which role (player or coach) would truly be high leverage. DO: Choose only 1 focus at a time, for specific period of time. Not two, just one. Decide what role you'll concentrate energy on, and for what period. Called "Fluid Focus." Example: Focus on coach role to help team navigate reorg - set aside 1 month. Intentionally place IC engineering work in back seat for that month. Accept trade - acknowledge won't get much (if any) progress on coding during that month. That's okay. Player role is something you'll come back to. To perform exceptionally well in one role rather than subpar in both, must pick one role to focus on at a time. Can switch focus later (Fluid Focus). However, none of this works if don't communicate it. DO NOT: Assume can put head down and just get job done. May seem great idea to focus purely on coach role for 1 month. But if team thinks receiving your code was top priority, they'll be confused why you haven't shipped it. If supervisor had deadline for code in mind, they'll believe you dropped the ball. DO: Communicate exactly the role you're prioritizing and why - with both team and supervisor. While may seem obvious to you, no one will know unless you tell them. Detail: (1) Role you're prioritizing (player or coach), (2) Period of time you'll be prioritizing that role, (3) Why you're prioritizing this role. Example to team: "For next 3 weeks, shifting priorities to focus on creation of new marketing campaign. As result, we'll pause our 1:1 meetings and team check-ins. May feel like I'm more absent than usual, but merely short phase to serve company's overall goals. Company's growth targets require I knock this out now, need focus deep work time. You can expect me to come out of this phase in 3 weeks, pick back up our 1:1 meetings again." Example to supervisors: "For next 3 weeks, thinking it makes sense to shift priorities to focus on completing creation of new marketing campaign - that way can hit deadline you intended for Q2 growth goals. Are you okay with me prioritizing this? If so, some managerial updates I usually send will be on pause for those 3 weeks, but happy to resume after phase completed. How does that sound?" For communication to supervisors in particular, actively asking for input on prioritization - ensures aligned with expectations, not superseding what supervisor believes most critical. DO NOT: Underestimate cost of context switching. Little wonder we're so tired as leaders. Context-switching consumes energy. Studies found 40% of productivity lost when context-switching. Accept that switching of context itself is work. Build in margin for that energy expenditure. Perhaps place 1 week buffer between modes as "catch up" time, where don't have specific focus on player or coach role. Gives time to evaluate which role might be best to focus on in next phase. Or maybe carve out break and take time off. DO NOT: Stay in 1 lane for too long... because you like it. Be wary of personal preferences for allocating time as leader. We all have bias to mode that's more "fun." For some, that's relishing being coach - helping team solve problems, coaching careers. For others, most comfortable jamming in deep work of player role. What we personally like is not always what situation requires. Must be truthful with ourselves to not conflate the two. The 4 Steps of Fluid Focus: (1) Stop chasing "balance." Commit to making trade. (2) Choose 1 role to focus on (player or coach) and period of time. (3) Communicate this choice to team and supervisor. (4) Readjust and reassess as needed. Inconvenient truth: Choice isn't obvious. Excruciatingly, almost impossibly hard to decide on just one role. Part of you wishes could "cheat" and do both roles at once. But remind yourself of toothpaste tube: Reason so exhausted and displeased is because squeezing yourself from both sides. Trying to do too many things at once. Embrace trade-off. Relish freedom to be fluid in focus. Side effects: Cognitive load of doing two roles at once slowly relieved. Find depth in work, not just surface area covered. Actual output of work more satisfactory. Reference to Andy Grove's "High Output Management": "My day always ends when I'm tired and ready to go home, not when I'm done. I'm never done." Managers have endless task list - leads to multi-tasking path. Worst part is feeling: mediocre at everything, great at nothing. Power of mono-tasking: choosing just one thing to do, and doing it well.

### Does Your Organization Have a Security.txt File?

- **Source:** Krebs on Security
- **Author:** Brian Krebs (Cybersecurity journalist and investigative reporter)
- **URL:** https://krebsonsecurity.com/2021/09/does-your-organization-have-a-security-txt-file/
- **Date:** 2025
- **Key Concepts:** Security.txt, vulnerability disclosure, security operations, internet standards, IETF, bug bounty programs, security contact information, operational security, cybersecurity
- **Key Insight:** Proposed Internet standard (not yet official IETF) that helps organizations describe vulnerability disclosure practices and preferences. Problem: Organizations get hacked because no obvious way for security researchers to report vulnerabilities or data leaks. Idea: Place file called security.txt in predictable place (example.com/security.txt or example.com/.well-known/security.txt). Contents vary but typically include: links to vulnerability disclosure policies, contact email address, links to bug bounty programs, public encryption keys, links thanking researchers, links to IT security job openings. Examples: USAA includes bug bounty program links, email, encryption key, disclosure policy, researcher thank you page. HCA Healthcare: contact email, responsible disclosure policies link, security job openings link. Why it matters: Makes easier to respond to active security threats. Real-world example: Major clothing retailer had VPN credentials stolen, no security.txt file, security@ email blocked, message to CIO - no way to know if read. Alex Holden (Hold Security): Malicious hackers often struggle getting attention of proper people in organizations they hacked. "In cases of ransom, bad guys try to contact company with demands. You have no idea how often their messages get caught in filters, get deleted, blocked or ignored." Many organizations unofficially use security@[companydomain] but not standardized or well-publicized, can be blocked. Adoption: Not yet official IETF standard, but 8% of Fortune 100 adopted (Alphabet, Amazon, Facebook, HCA Healthcare, Kroger, Procter & Gamble, USAA, Walmart). Major downside: Setting up security.txt invites high volume of spam. Self-appointed penetration testers run automated vulnerability discovery tools, submit reports hoping for consulting engagement or bug bounty fee. Hacker News threads discuss being flooded with low-quality vulnerability scan reports, difficult to spot reports worth pursuing. Edwin "EdOverflow" Foudil (co-author of proposed standard) acknowledges: "Organizations that implement this are going to get flooded. One reason bug bounty programs succeed is they are basically glorified spam filter. But regardless of approach, going to get inundated with crappy, sub-par reports." Often from individuals who scanned entire Internet for one or two vulnerabilities, contacted all vulnerable organizations at once semi-automated. Managing noise: Many nuisance reports can be ignored or grouped by creating filters looking for keywords commonly found in automated vulnerability scans. Success stories: Tremendous feedback from universities that implemented security.txt. "Incredible success with universities, which tend to have lots of older, legacy systems. In that context, seen ton of valuable reports." Origin: Came about after chatting with people at DEFCON (annual security conference in Las Vegas) struggling to report security issues to vendors. Foudil not trying to make money off it. Main reason not promoting heavily: not yet official standard. Key takeaways: (1) Standardized way for organizations to publish vulnerability disclosure practices/contact info, (2) Solves real problem - lack of clear, predictable way for researchers to report vulnerabilities, (3) Trade-offs - easier for legitimate researchers but generates high volume of low-quality reports, (4) Spam can be managed with email filters/keyword filtering, (5) Particularly valuable for organizations with legacy systems (universities), (6) Not yet official but adopted by major Fortune 100 companies, suggesting practical value before formal IETF approval.

### SOC2: The Screenshots Will Continue Until Security Improves

- **Source:** Fly.io Blog
- **Author:** Thomas Ptacek (@tqbf)
- **URL:** https://fly.io/blog/soc2-the-screenshots-will-continue-until-security-improves/
- **Date:** 2025
- **Key Concepts:** SOC2, compliance, security audit, DRL, Type I, Type II, SSO, access control, vulnerability management, security policies, operational security, security certification
- **Key Insight:** Practical guide to SOC2 compliance from someone who project-managed Fly.io's SOC2 certification. Bottom line for customers: SOC2 is weak positive indicator of security maturity, similar ballpark to penetration test report (but less than multiple pentests). Key distinctions: (1) SOC2 is accounting-style audit confirming on-paper claims about security processes, not like adversarial security audits/penetration tests. (2) About security of company (can customer support pop shell on prod?), not products (can attacker pop shell with SQL injection?). (3) Guts are giant spreadsheet questionnaire (DRL) and battery of screenshots as evidence. (4) DRL is high-level/abstract, product of accounting standards like COSO framework, understands "git" and "MFA" but nothing lower-level. (5) Two types: Type I (point-in-time snapshot), Type II (confirms consistent adherence over months). Can't flunk Type I but Type II more annoying - can't travel back in time to close gaps. What SOC2 assures that pentests don't: consistent access policies, everyone has 2FA, capability to fire alerts on weird logs, severed employees reliably have access terminated, company not run by 4 raccoons in trenchcoat (or policy written accepting risk). For customers: Intensely technical/high-risk engineering? Look for pentest. Huge number of employees? Get SOC2. What's SOC2: Best-known infosec certification, only one routinely demanded by customers. Structure: AICPA "Trust Services Criteria" and COSO framework. Categories: security (mandatory, only one matters for most), availability, transaction integrity, confidentiality, privacy. DRL = Document Request List - giant spreadsheet auditor customizes to your company after scoping call. Can't reason back to first principles - just take their word for it. When to SOC2: Wait as long as you can. Important distinction: "Getting SOC2-certified" â‰  "doing engineering work to get SOC2-certified". Do engineering now, as early as possible - work scales with team size. Audit itself doesn't matter except to answer customer request "can I have your SOC2 report?" Do it when more economical to get certification than hand-hold prospects through security process. Why customers ask: It's viral like GPL. DRL suggests policy to "collect SOC2 reports from all vendors". Customers ask because they're SOC2 and AICPA wants them to force you to join coven. Don't have to do it - can speak confidently about security practice or pay audit firm to make problem go away. Makes little sense to get certified before customers demand it. Can make purchase order contingent on Type I in next year. What SOC2 made Fly.io do: (1) Single Sign-On - every CSO says SSO was first 3 things. Obvious why: single place to control credentials (phishing-proof MFA), access to apps, offboarding. Moved everything to Google SSO. Inside network: certificate-based SSH access control (Teleport). To reach Teleport need VPN, to get VPN need Google SSO. Teleport authenticated separately via Github SSO. Two auth sources of truth for SSH. Teleport killer feature: transcript-level audit logs for every SSH session, can watch other team sessions, go back in time. "SSO tax" - pain to pay for SAAS accounts supporting SAML/OIDC. Early days can skip for non-sensitive vendors, but mostly just pay. (2) Protected Branches - surprisingly important to auditors. Concern: developer might "go rogue" and install malware. Easy to enable in Github but also need documented approval process ensuring changes reviewed. Fly.io wasn't doing this - have teams-of-one components. Solution: Post-facto reviews. Regular reviews on large components, smaller projects/urgent fixes can merge unreviewed (by authorized subset), Github bot flags PRs, weekly meeting to review. (3) Centralized Logging - big chunk of DRL about monitoring. Auditors nudge toward centralizing (but you want this anyway - every logging system is one to document/screenshot/write policy about). Fly.io got off easy - logging is platform feature, large ElasticSearch/VictoriaMetrics clusters, single query away from any event. SOC2 forced picking ticketing system (had avoided for years). Alerts to Slack/PagerDuty, documented ticketing processes. HelpScout surprise: got lots of SOC2 mileage - support/security@ mails go there, good system of record for events SOC2 cares about (internal/external security reports). (4) CloudTrail - barely use AWS but audit firms added AWS-specific line-items. Better at indexing/alerting CloudTrail now, not very useful to their security practice. (5) Infrastructure-as-Code - auditors know Terraform/CloudFormation, want you using it. Job is documenting how your deployment system is similar to Terraform. Host inventory annoyance: trivial for AWS (API/CLI), Fly.io runs own hardware, systems of record are idiosyncratic, ended up taking screenshots of SQL queries. (6) MDM/Endpoint Management - surprise: endpoint security has fallen out of fashion in 2022. Didn't have to document endpoint strategy. Can cross off checklist. (7) Boring Company Stuff - (a) Formal org chart posted where employees can find it. Annoying but whatever. (b) Evidence of annual performance reviews. Fly.io doesn't do annual (continuous feedback, 1-on-1 culture). AICPA can't assure terminated employee if no annual reviews. So now have pro-forma annual reviews. Need to carefully explain to team so don't think stack ranking. (c) Background checks - performative/intrusive, occasionally illegal (European employees), horror stories about flagging for missing transcripts. Only issue had to seriously back-and-forth with auditors. Held line on refusing, got out of it by finding what another company did, stealing process, telling auditors "do what you did for them". (8) Policies - going to write bunch. Most companies phone it in, Google template, adopt first result. Fly.io wrote their own. Simplified by Ryan McGeehan's "Starting Up Security" site (free). Information Security Policy based on Ryan's, all their own words (quotes grugq). Writing policies inspired by Ryan liberates you to write clearly, no "whereas" or "designee". Wrote: Information Security Policy (everyone signs), Data Classification Policy (what can go in Slack/Email vs talk to management), Document Retention policy (from lawyers), Change Management, Risk Assessment (says will build spreadsheet explaining zombie apocalypse handling), Vulnerability Management (roughly explains nmap), Access Request (which Slack channel), Vendor Management (propagates SOC2 virus to vendors), Incident Response (cribbed from Ryan), Business Continuity (Jerome in charge if Kurt arrested for robbing bank), employee handbook. Slab verification feature: highest ROI feature - click button, adds green banner saying "verified" for next several months. Several DRL items about recertifying policies annually - this gave screenshots needed. What Fly.io didn't let SOC2 make them do: (1) Install endpoint software, (2) Run vulnerability scanner (automated nmap for compliance), (3) Collect SOC2 reports from all vendors (will do soon-ish), (4) Clear Prototype Injection alerts from Dependabot for Javascript projects, (5) Install agent software on servers (terrified of agents), (6) Run antivirus on servers (explained why doesn't make sense - documented deployment/CI/CD), (7) Run any other security product (IDS, WAF, SEM, DAST, ASM, XDR, CASB, anything with "mesh"). The Audit Itself: People joke about screenshots - they're not joking. Whole SOC2 audit is essentially series of screenshots. Jarring if you've had security audits by consulting firms (technical experts jump into codebase, try to find flaws). Nothing like that in SOC2 - it's actual audit. DRL line items ask for evidence supporting claims (e.g. "production repos on Github have protected branches"). Evidence almost always screenshots of management interface with feature enabled. Divided evidence collection into series of calls over week, each ~20 minutes, mostly sharing screen saying "that checkbox over there, screenshot that". Prepared for tricky calls, but was chill process. Most important thing: Keep goals modest. Claims you make in Type I bind on Type II; claims you don't make in Type I won't. One Type I goal should be helping future self clear Type II. About report quality: Concerned quality would matter to customers. Got good auditors, wasn't paint-by-numbers. But talking to security people at other companies, takeaway: having SOC2 report is what matters, not what it says. The five Type I reports trick: Peer at highly clueful security-sensitive firm described vendor giving them five consecutive Type I reports. Can skip all real work! (Spent weeks convincing CEO not to do this.) What SOC2 misses: Fly.io does lots of security work SOC2 doesn't care about - misses most of their security model. Build in memory-safe languages, minimize trust between components, simple access control models, get code pentested. SOC2 doesn't communicate this - and that's fine. Can write own security report explaining what they do. For all cynicism, SOC2 dragged them into process improvements they're glad to have. Helped to have clueful auditors, clear eye about what trying to accomplish. Expected "entire new cloud provider" to be complicated case but went smoothly. If easy for them, probably easier for you. Just don't do it until you have to.

These habits often feel like strengths or are so ingrained we don't notice them, but they can undermine team morale, make others feel undervalued, create cultures where people are afraid to speak up, prevent seeing better ideas/solutions, limit ability to grow/adapt. The 20 habits: (1) Winning too much - need to win at all costs in all situations, even when it doesn't matter. (2) Adding value - overwhelming desire to add two cents to every discussion, even when input not needed. (3) Passing judgment - need to rate others and impose standards on them. (4) Making destructive comments - needless sarcasms and cutting remarks we think make us sound sharp/witty, often undermine others. (6) Telling the world how smart you are - need to show people we're smarter than they think, constantly demonstrating intelligence rather than letting actions speak. (8) Negativity, or "Let me explain why that won't work" - need to share negative thoughts even when not asked, automatically pointing out problems rather than solutions. (9) Withholding information - refusal to share information to maintain advantage over others, keeping knowledge to maintain power/control. (10) Failing to give proper recognition - inability to praise and reward, not acknowledging others' contributions. (11) Claiming credit that we don't deserve - most annoying way to overestimate contribution to success, taking credit for work/ideas that weren't yours. (12) Making excuses - need to reposition annoying behavior as permanent fixture so people excuse us, rationalizing why we can't/shouldn't have to change. (13) Clinging to the past - need to deflect blame onto events/people from past, subset of blaming everyone else, living in past rather than present/future. (14) Playing favorites - failing to see treating someone unfairly, showing preference for certain people without recognizing inequity. (15) Refusing to express regret - inability to take responsibility, admit wrong, recognize how actions affect others, unwilling/unable to apologize. (16) Not listening - most passive-aggressive form of disrespect, being present but not hearing, making others feel unheard. (17) Failing to express gratitude - most basic form of bad manners, not saying thank you or showing appreciation. (18) Punishing the messenger - misguided need to attack innocent who are trying to help, shooting messenger when receive bad news rather than addressing problem. (19) Passing the buck - need to blame everyone but ourselves, never taking responsibility, always finding someone else to hold accountable. (20) An excessive need to be "me" - exalting faults as virtues because "that's who we are," refusing to change problematic behaviors rather than recognizing growth requires change. Why these habits matter: They worked in earlier stages of career - being competitive, adding value, showing intelligence, being critical helped stand out. But as move into leadership, these behaviors can undermine team morale/collaboration, make others feel undervalued/dismissed, create cultures where people afraid to speak up, prevent seeing better ideas/solutions, limit ability to grow/adapt. Self-assessment: Look at list and honestly assess which habits you recognize in yourself. Most successful people find several. Goal isn't to identify every single one but recognize patterns holding you back. Challenge: Habits often feel like strengths or so ingrained we don't notice. They become part of identity. But recognizing them is first step toward change.

### Porter's Generic Strategies

- **Source:** Wikipedia
- **Author:** Various contributors (based on Michael Porter's work)
- **URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porter%27s_generic_strategies
- **Date:** 2025 (page continuously updated)
- **Original Sources:**
  - "Competitive Strategy" by Michael Porter (1980), Free Press
  - "Competitive Advantage" by Michael Porter (1985), Free Press
- **Key Concepts:** Competitive strategy, competitive advantage, cost leadership, differentiation, focus strategy, market scope, strategic management, Porter's framework
- **Key Insight:** Framework describing how companies can pursue competitive advantage across chosen market scope. Three generic strategies: cost leadership, product differentiation, and focus (with two variants - cost focus and differentiation focus - making four distinct approaches). Core insight: Company chooses one of two types of competitive advantage - lower costs than competition OR differentiation along dimensions valued by customers to command higher price. Company also chooses one of two types of scope - focus (selected segments) OR industry-wide (many segments). Generic strategy reflects choices regarding both type of competitive advantage and scope. Porter's original argument: Must choose only one strategy or risk "stuck in the middle" scenario. Practicing more than one strategy loses focus, clear direction can't be established. Differentiation incurs costs contradicting low cost strategy. Standardized products won't carry differentiation. Cost leadership and differentiation mutually exclusive - two focal objectives clash. Empirical basis: "Hole in the middle" problem - firms with high market share (cost leaders) and low market share (niche players) profitable, firms with moderate market share (stuck in middle) least profitable. Exception: Combining market segmentation with product differentiation effective. Combinations like cost leadership with differentiation hard (but not impossible) due to conflict between cost minimization and value-added differentiation costs. Cost Leadership Strategy: Target customers in most/all segments based on lowest price. Win market share by appealing to cost-conscious/price-sensitive customers. Lowest prices or lowest price-to-value ratio. Must operate at lower cost than rivals. Three ways: (1) High asset utilization - service industries turn tables/flights fast, manufacturing high volume output. Fixed costs spread over more units = lower unit cost. Economies of scale/experience curve effects. High output requires/creates high market share, entry barrier. (2) Low direct/indirect operating costs - high volumes standardized products, basic no-frills, limit customization/personalization. Production costs low via fewer components, standard components, limit models. Overheads low via low wages, low rent areas, cost-conscious culture. Continuous cost reduction search - outsourcing, controlling production, increasing utilization, minimizing distribution/R&D/advertising. Extensive distribution. Promotional strategy makes virtue of low cost features. (3) Control over value chain - all functional groups (finance, supply/procurement, marketing, inventory, IT) ensure low costs. Supply chain: bulk buying/quantity discounts, squeeze suppliers, competitive bidding, low inventories (JIT, VMI). Example: Wal-Mart squeezing suppliers. Preferential raw materials access, backward integration. Viable for large firms with economies of scale/large volumes/big market share. Small businesses can be "cost focused" not "cost leaders" if have low cost advantages. Example: Local restaurant low rent location, limited menu, rapid turnover, minimum wage staff. Innovation may enable startup/small company offer cheaper product where incumbents too expensive. Example: Low-cost budget airlines - fewer planes but market share growth via cheap no-frills services. Started with cost focused, grew to cost leadership. Disadvantages: Lower customer loyalty (price-sensitive switch when substitute available), reputation for low quality (hard to rebrand/shift to differentiation). Differentiation Strategy: Target customers in most/all segments based on attributes other than price (quality/service) to command higher price. Differentiate favorably relative to competition. Minimize costs in areas that don't differentiate, remain cost competitive. Appropriate when: target segment not price-sensitive, competitive/saturated market, customers have specific under-served needs, firm has unique resources/capabilities difficult to copy. Unique resources: patents/IP, unique technical expertise (Apple design, Pixar animation), talented personnel (sports stars, star traders), innovative processes. Success when: premium price, increased revenue per unit, brand loyalty. Works when added price outweighs added expense, ineffective when uniqueness easily replicated. Brand management creates perceived uniqueness even when physical product same (Chiquita bananas, Starbucks coffee, Nike sneakers, fashion brands). Examples: Hero, Asian Paints, HUL, Nike, BMW, Perstorp BioProducts, Apple, Mercedes-Benz. Not suitable for small companies. Appropriate for big companies applying in one/several functional groups. Example: GE uses finance division to differentiate. May do in isolation or conjunction with focus strategies (requires more investment). Good advantage using differentiation with focus cost/differentiation strategies. Example: Coca-Cola and Royal Crown. Variants: Shareholder value model - timing of specialized knowledge creates differentiation advantage while unique. Customers buy for access to unique knowledge. Advantage static (one-time purchase). Unlimited resources model - greater resources manage risk/sustain profits more easily. Short-term advantage only. Without continual innovation capacity, won't sustain competitive position. Note: Henry Mintzberg argues cost leadership is form of differentiation using lower price. Focus Strategies: Focusing on one or few segments. May offer lower cost (cost focus) OR differentiate (differentiation focus) in that scope. Not separate strategy for big companies due to small market conditions. Big companies applying differentiation may also use focus strategies. Definitely appropriate for small companies, especially wanting to avoid competition with big ones. Narrow focus on few target markets (segmentation/niche strategy). Distinct groups with specialized needs. Choice of low prices or differentiated products/services depends on segment needs and firm resources/capabilities. Focus marketing on one-two narrow segments, tailor marketing mix. Gain competitive advantage through product innovation/brand marketing rather than efficiency. Target segments less vulnerable to substitutes or where competition weakest for above-average ROI. Example: Southwest Airlines - short-haul point-to-point vs hub-and-spoke of United/American. Subsequent Developments: Treacy and Wiersema (1993) modified Porter's three strategies in "The Discipline of Market Leaders" to three "value disciplines": operational excellence, product leadership, customer intimacy. Criticisms/Hybrid Strategy Debate: Several commentators questioned generic strategies - lack specificity, flexibility, limiting, empirical challenges. Miller questions "caught in the middle" - viable middle ground exists. Many companies enter as niche player and expand. Baden-Fuller and Stopford: most successful companies resolve "dilemma of opposites." Reeves and Routledge (2013): entrepreneurial spirit key factor in success, differentiation/cost leadership least important. Contemporary research shows evidence of successful "hybrid strategy." Davis (1984): firms employing hybrid (low cost + differentiation) outperform ones adopting one generic strategy. Hill (1988): challenged mutual exclusivity, argued successful combination results in sustainable competitive advantage. Wright (1990): multiple strategies required to respond effectively to environment. Mid-late 1980s environments stable - no flexibility requirement. Present market contexts rapidly changing/highly unpredictable - require flexibility for contingencies. After 11 years Porter revised thinking and accepted hybrid business strategy could exist. Why hybrid necessary: Highly volatile/turbulent markets don't permit rigid strategies. Long-term establishment depends on agility/quick responsiveness. Market/environmental turbulence makes drastic implications on firm establishment. If strategy can't cope with contingencies, long-term survival unrealistic. Pragmatic approach: Diverging strategy into different avenues to exploit opportunities/avoid threats. Consistent superior performance possible with hybrid strategy - stronger foundations. Depending on market/competitive conditions, hybrid should be adjusted regarding extent each generic strategy (cost leadership or differentiation) given priority. Key Takeaways: Three fundamental strategic options - cost leadership, differentiation, focus. Choice matters - Porter originally argued must choose one or risk "stuck in middle." Context matters - appropriateness depends on firm size, market conditions, resources. Debate continues - framework influential but empirical evidence/practical experience suggest hybrid strategies successful, especially volatile markets. Flexibility key - rapidly changing markets may require combining strategies or adjusting emphasis. Small vs large - focus appropriate for small companies, cost leadership requires scale.

### Can Do Vs Must Do

- **Source:** AVC Blog
- **Author:** Fred Wilson (Co-founder and partner at Union Square Ventures)
- **URL:** https://avc.com/2017/05/can-do-vs-must-do/
- **Date:** 2025
- **Key Concepts:** Prioritization, focus, strategy, execution, resource management, leadership, rule of three, must-do vs can-do, startup management, saying no
- **Key Insight:** The difference between "can do" and "must do" projects, and how companies often confuse the two. With abundance of capital, portfolio companies have resources to do more than they can and should do. They greenlight "can do" projects but not "must do" projects. Problems when confusing the two: (1) Core resources (infrastructure, security, payments, design, product management) get stretched supporting many efforts, (2) Team loses sight of mission and strategy as so many projects tackled simultaneously, (3) Senior leadership gets pulled in many directions and loses alignment, (4) Projects slip or don't ship, leading to malaise and morale issues, (5) Headcount grows quickly to support efforts, creating management issues. Test all initiatives against "must do" vs "can do" test. Just because you can do something doesn't mean you should. Strategy is about saying no: Strategy isn't saying no. It's figuring out what is most important for company and deciding to focus on it and say no to everything else. To figure out what's most important, need to understand: products, customers, market position, where things are going, where you want to be in 3-5 years. Once figured out, determine most important things needed to get there. The startup as video game metaphor: Partner Albert thinks doing startup is like playing video game. Might mean parting ways with people you don't need - painful but helpful. What good execution looks like: Executing well on all "must do" things is hallmark of well-run company. Usually means there aren't many "can do" things on roadmap. Can only do few things well, must be most critical. Time is finite, can't keep adding priorities. Each addition requires removing or deprioritizing something else. The people challenge: People who support "can do" instead of "must do" are frequently closest to you. They're encouraging, see possibilities, want to be helpful. But those are people you need to disappoint most acutely. Frameworks for deciding: Alternative lens is resources and reusability. Three questions: (1) How much do we risk if we don't do it? (2) If we do it, will it enrich platform for everyone? (3) What will it take to deliver? The life application: Applies to business or life. To be successful, say no a lot. To be really successful, say no almost all the time. Don't spread yourself too thin. Time is finite. Activity vs Momentum: Two types of organizations - "Activity" organizations (going nowhere) and "Momentum" organizations (know what they must do and do it well). Keeping main thing the main thing: "The main thing is keeping the main thing the main thing" - Stephen Covey. Regular review: What's main thing? Is everything supporting it? Most companies misunderstand differences between strategy, tactics, objectives. Key takeaways: (1) "Can do" is not same as "must do" - just because have resources doesn't mean should, (2) Rule of three - don't tackle more than three big things at once, (3) Strategy is focus - figuring out what's most important and saying no to everything else, (4) Startup is video game - each level requires mastering one thing, (5) Good execution requires focus - executing well on "must dos" is hallmark of well-run company, (6) Time is finite - can only do few things well, (7) Say no to say yes - path to excellence requires saying no to many "can dos" to say yes to "must dos", (8) Cost of confusion - mixing leads to stretched resources, lost alignment, slipped projects, malaise.

### Good Engineering Strategy Is Boring

- **Source:** Irrational Exuberance (lethain.com)
- **Author:** Will Larson (Author of "An Elegant Puzzle," "Staff Engineer," "The Engineering Executive's Primer," and "Crafting Engineering Strategy")
- **URL:** https://lethain.com/good-engineering-strategy-is-boring/
- **Date:** 2025
- **Key Concepts:** Engineering strategy, engineering vision, design documents, RFCs, tech specs, bottoms-up organizational learning, strategy synthesis, tradeoffs, decision-making
- **Key Insight:** Practical, bottoms-up approach to writing engineering strategy and vision. Core insight: Good engineering strategy is boring, and it's easier to write an effective strategy than a bad one. The process: (1) Write five design documents describing decisions and tradeoffs in specific projects. Design docs have what bad strategies lack - detailed specifics grounded in reality. It's easy for two engineers to interpret abstract strategy differently, but harder to stay misaligned when implementing specific solution. When to write design docs: projects used by numerous future projects, projects meaningfully impacting users, work taking more than month of engineering time. Best practices: Start from problem (clearer problem = more obvious solutions), keep template simple (minimal templates, select useful sections, exhaustive details only for riskiest), gather and review together/write alone (collect input widely but one author writes clearer document than group edits), prefer good over perfect (better to get good document in front of others than delay). To improve: reread designs after implementation, study where implementation deviated from plan. (2) Synthesize five design docs into strategy - read all five together, look for controversial decisions that came up in multiple designs, particularly those hard to agree on. Example: debating whether Redis appropriate as durable storage or only cache - write down recent decisions and rationale as strategy. Good strategies guide tradeoffs and explain rationale. Bad strategies state policy without explanation, decoupling from context. Strategy definition = "diagnosis" and "guiding policies" (deferring "coherent action" to design documents). Best practices: Start where you are (dive in, waiting for missing information doesn't work, whatever you write will need to change), write specifics (write until you generalize then stop, specific statements create alignment, generic create illusion), be opinionated (provide clarity on decision making), show your work (explain rationale so others can modify/extend as context shifts). Some best strategies feel too obvious to write - "When should we write design documents?", "Which databases for which use cases?", "Which services page during off-hours" are all worth writing. (3) Extrapolate five strategies into vision - as collect more strategies, becomes challenging to reason about how strategies interact. Take five recent strategies, extrapolate how tradeoffs will play out over next 2-3 years. Edit through contradictions, weave threads together = engineering vision. Gives "robust belief in the future" (Tanya Reilly) making it easier to understand how existing strategies relate, simplifies writing new strategies. Best practices: Write 2-3 years out (companies/tech change quickly, too far is fraught, too short doesn't work), ground in business and users (tight connection keeps vision aligned with leadership values, bad visions treat technical sophistication as self-justifying), be optimistic not audacious (ambitious but possible, best possible version of possible, write what could accomplish if projects finished on time, don't write infinite resources version), stay concrete and specific (more useful as more specific, generic easy to agree with but don't help reconcile conflicts), keep 1-2 pages long (most people don't read long documents, compact with links to extra context). Managing expectations: Response to vision usually muted - core audience is folks writing strategies (small cohort), great vision is so obvious it bores more than excites. Don't measure by initial excitement. Measure by reading design document from two years ago vs last week - if marked improvement, vision is good. When and why: Strategies are tools of proactive alignment, empower teams to move quickly with confidence, allow everyone to make quick confident decisions (would otherwise cost week of discussion). Write strategy when you've rehashed same discussion 3-4 times. Write vision when future's too hazy to identify investments worth making. If neither sound familiar, move on to other work. The prework: If can't resist urge to include brilliant ideas, write all best ideas into giant document, delete it, never mention again. Clears head for work ahead - process should be grounded in what you've actually done, not what you wish you'd done. Key principle: Durably useful engineering strategy and vision are output of iterative, bottoms-up organizational learning. All learning contributes to strategy and vision, but contribution doesn't have to be abstract. Even if not directly responsible, can take practical steps to advance organization's strategy and vision starting right now.

### Is Engineering Strategy Useful?

- **Source:** Irrational Exuberance (lethain.com)
- **Author:** Will Larson (Author of "An Elegant Puzzle," "Staff Engineer," "The Engineering Executive's Primer," and "Crafting Engineering Strategy")
- **URL:** https://lethain.com/is-engineering-strategy-useful/
- **Date:** 2025
- **Original Source:** This is a chapter from "Crafting Engineering Strategy"
- **Key Concepts:** Engineering strategy, written strategy, implicit strategy, organizational learning, strategy impact, strategy costs, personal development, strategy value
- **Key Insight:** Why engineering strategy matters and why written strategy is valuable. Core insight: There's always an engineering strategy, even if nothing written down. Strategy embedded into organization's decisions, although in many organizations only visible to small group and may be quickly forgotten. If thinking strategy doesn't exist, ask where strategy lives if can't find it. May find it ineffective, but never found that it doesn't exist. Strategy is impactful: Creating alignment (example: Calm's engineering strategy reduced friction by eliminating cause of ongoing debate, reset team focus, caused some engineers to leave because incompatible with priorities - not downside, clear documented strategy let everyone accurately decide if wanted to be part of game). Ways strategies support organizations: (1) Concentrating company investment into smaller space (e.g., deciding not to decompose monolith allows investing majority of tooling on one language, one test suite, one deployment mechanism), (2) Many interesting properties only available through universal adoption (e.g., "N-1 policy" on backfilled roles significant opportunity for managing costs but only works if consistently adopted, disaster recovery/multi-region strategies only viable if all infrastructure has common configuration mechanism), (3) Focus execution on what truly matters (e.g., Stripe's Sorbet strategy allowed team of ten engineers to incrementally move Ruby monolith towards static typing without distracting larger organization, could have consumed entire organization for many months but focus allowed small team accomplish majority of early work), (4) Creating knowledge repository of how organization thinks (onboarding new hires, particularly senior, much more effective with documented strategy, e.g., strategy for accessing user data requires clear user-understandable rationale for access - obvious to new hires from larger companies but folks with only small company experience likely completely unaware necessary, if not documented compliance quickly declines). However developing strategy creates progress even if progress is understanding inherent disagreement. Inappropriate strategy especially impactful: While good strategy accomplishes many things, inappropriate strategy feels far more impactful (in wrong ways). Example: Digg V4 - complete rewrite from PHP monolith to PHP frontend and backend of dozen Python services, moved database from sharded MySQL to early version of Cassandra, replaced nuanced algorithms developed over decade with hack implemented few days before launch. Although Digg likely would have struggled to become profitable due to reliance on SEO for traffic and Google's frequently changing search algorithm, engineering strategy ensured died fast rather than having opportunity to dig way out. Not just Digg - almost every engineering organization has unused platform projects that captured decades of engineering years to detriment of important opportunity. Shocking number of senior leaders join new companies and initiate grand migration attempting to entirely rewrite architecture, switch programming languages, or shift organization to resemble prior organization where understood things better. Term "inappropriate" rather than "bad" - same strategy might be very effective in different circumstances (e.g., if Digg had been three person company with no revenue, rewriting from scratch could have been right decision). Every approach undoubtedly works in some organization. Written strategy drives organizational learning: Example Carta - inconsistent approach to important problems, teams had distinct standard kits, adoption of existing internal platforms inconsistent, decision making around funding new internal platforms inconsistent, widespread agreement decomposing monolith but no agreement on how. Coming into permissive strategy environment with strong differing perspectives, wrote explicit engineering strategy with Navigators team (explicitly named technical leaders representing key parts of engineering organization - representative leadership group made possible to iterate on strategy with small team of about ten engineers representing entire organization rather than negotiate with 400 engineers directly). Written strategy made possible to explicitly describe problems seen and how wanted to navigate them, artifact able to iterate on in small group then share widely for feedback, could revise and update over time (e.g., extending to mention AI/LLMs, then revising again as dive into agentic workflows). Disagreement is feature - lot of people disagreed with parts, great benefit of written strategy is possible to precisely disagree, from disagreement able to evolve strategy (sometimes new information, other times initial approach could be improved). New hires able to disagree from informed place rather than coming across attached to prior company's practices, able to understand historical thinking motivating decisions even when context no longer obvious. With oral history, what you believe highly dependent on who you talk with, shapes view of history and present. With written history, far more possible to agree at scale, prerequisite to growing at scale rather than isolating growth to small pockets of senior leadership. Cost of implicit strategy: Vulnerable to misinterpretation - information flow in verbal organizations depends on individual being in room for decision then accurately repeating to others who need it, common to see fail to repeat elsewhere or interpretation faulty, both create significant problems. "Two-headed organizations" solution - two leaders (manager and senior engineer) to reduce communication errors, errors in point-to-point communication so prevalent one-to-one that only solution for non-reading-oriented communicators was ensuring communicated strategy to at least two people. Inconsistency across teams - example: promotions to Staff-plus role happened at much higher rate in infrastructure engineering than product engineering, created constant drain out of product engineering to work on infrastructure-shaped problems even if not valuable to business, new leaders had no idea informal policy existed, routinely ran into trouble in calibration discussions, also weren't aware needed to argue for better policy, worse no one sure if real policy or not so ultimately random whether perspective represented for any promotion (sometimes good promotions blocked, sometimes borderline cases approved). Inconsistency over time - implementing new policy mix of persistent and one-time actions, e.g., standardize all HTTP operations to use same library across codebase (add linter check, one-time pass), but two years later another three random HTTP libraries in codebase creeping into cracks. If policy written down and few people read it, number of ways could be prevented. If not written down, much less likely someone remembers, much less likely remember rationale well enough to argue about it. Hazard to new leadership - when new Staff-plus engineer or executive joins, common to blame them for failing to understand existing context behind decisions (fair: big part of senior leadership uncovering and understanding context, unfair: explicit documentation would have made much easier). Every particularly bad new-leader onboarding involved new leader coming into unfilled role that manager didn't know how to do, success entirely dependent on new leader's ability and interest in learning. Practice of documenting strategy has lot in common with succession planning - full benefits accrue to organization rather than individual doing it, possible to maintain when original authors present, appreciating value requires stepping outside yourself to value things that matter most to organization when you're no longer member. Information herd immunity - frequent objection "no one reads anything", some truth but extremely hard to get everyone to know something, never found that goal particularly important. View of information dispersal same as herd immunity: don't need everyone to know something, just enough people who know something that confusion doesn't propagate too far. Impossible for all engineers to know strategy details, but can have every Staff-plus engineer and engineering manager know those details. Strategy supports personal learning: While largest benefits accrue to organization, strategy is underrated avenue for self-development. (1) Creating strategy builds self-awareness - example: engineers viewing themselves extremely senior but frequently demanded projects implemented using new programming languages/technologies because personally wanted to learn (internal strategy clear - wanted to work on something fun), but following steps to build engineering strategy would have created strategy even they agreed didn't make sense. (2) Strategy supports situational awareness in new environments - Wardley mapping talks about situational awareness as prerequisite to good strategy (ensuring understand realities of circumstances, most destructive failure of new senior engineering leaders), by explicitly stating diagnosis where strategy applied makes easier to debug why reusing prior strategy in new team/company might not work. (3) Strategy as personal archive - documented strategy is institutional memory and personal memory to understand impact of prior approaches, each of us archivist of prior work pulling out valuable pieces to address problem at hand, over long career memory fades and motivated reasoning creeps in but explicit documentation doesn't. Part of reason started working on book now rather than later - realized starting to forget details of strategy work did earlier in career, if wanted to preserve wisdom of that era and ensure didn't have to relearn same lessons, had to write now. Summary: Two key themes most frequently ignored - (1) There's always a strategy even if isn't written down, (2) Single biggest act to further strategy in organization is write down strategy so can be debated, agreed upon, and explicitly evolved. Discussions around strategy often get caught up in high prestige activities like making controversial decisions, but most effective strategists make more progress by performing basics: writing things down, exploring widely to see how other companies solve same problem, accepting feedback into draft from folks who disagree. Strategy is useful, and doing strategy can be simple too.

### Follow Culture First, Don't Start by Changing It

- **Source:** Engineering Strategy (learnings.aleixmorgadas.dev)
- **Author:** Aleix Morgadas
- **URL:** https://learnings.aleixmorgadas.dev/p/do-not-start-by-changing-the-culture
- **Date:** 2025
- **Key Concepts:** Organizational culture, cultural change, middle management, engineering leadership, culture dimensions, organizational behavior, change management
- **Key Insight:** Start with small processes that eventually lead to cultural change, rather than trying to change culture directly. For middle management, following the culture is the path of least resistance to achieve goals. Core insight: Aiming to drive company cultural change as middle management is recipe for burnout. Instead, follow the culture - it is path for less resistance to achieve what you aim to achieve. Understanding culture: Culture refers to shared values, beliefs, attitudes, behaviors, and practices that define how business operates and how employees interact. Long story short: how decisions are made. Based on Tanya Reilly's "The Staff Engineer's Path," when people ask about culture they refer to: How much autonomy will I have? Will I feel included? Will it be safe to make mistakes? Will I be part of decisions that affect me? How difficult will it be to make progress on projects? Are people nice? Culture is how we behave in day to day, how we make decisions, and based on which values. Seven dimensions to describe culture (from Tanya Reilly's book): (1) Secret vs. Open - How much does everyone know? Information is currency? Calendars private? Slack channels invitation only? Everyone has access to everything? Even messy first drafts? (2) Oral vs. Written - What gets shared by word of mouth vs written down? How much writing and review involved in decisions? Features come with formal specifications or need to go to person who made feature? (3) Top-down vs. Bottom-up - Where do initiatives come from? Bottom-up culture where employees/teams empowered to make own decisions and champion initiatives they think important, but when need support they slow down. (4) Fast vs. Deliberate - Rapid decisions and pivot abruptly to try new opportunity, or need to show thought through whole plan before executing? (5) Back channels vs. Front doors - How do people in different groups talk? Formal path via teams' managers, or people can send friendly DMs cross teams? (6) Allocated vs. Available - How much time does everyone have? Teams understaffed or overworked? Able to absorb new work as appears, or ends in backlog needs prioritized in next cycle? (7) Liquid vs. Crystallized - Where do power, status, reputation come from? How gain trust? Clear hierarchy? In specific point in hierarchy, as others move up you also move? Or more Liquid, offers room for change, need to move yourself to be promoted. Understanding your culture first: Two common responses when asking leaders about culture: (1) "That's not the type of company I want to be part of," (2) "That's why we're not able to drive necessary change, culture isn't helping us, need to change it first so can do necessary changes later." Culture never black or white, grey scale in multiple levels. Describe differently for overall company culture vs tribe's vs team's. Two scenarios for improving culture: C-level (CTO/CIO) vs Middle management (VPs, Heads of Engineering, Engineering Managers, Staff/Principal Engineers). Focus on middle management where driving cultural change more challenging. The middle management challenge: When talking to middle managers and Principal Engineers, challenges often not about culture as goal to accomplish but as blocker to achieve desired outcome. Typical: "I want to drive initiative to accomplish A, but would be easier if had more Y culture. How can I influence leadership to change culture?" Answer: Don't aim to change culture. Cultural change takes time, requires constant leadership support, lot of patience. By time achieved that, challenge now will no longer matter. Instead, follow culture - path for less resistance. If approach/plan/strategy isn't possible due to cultural restrictions, problem is your approach not possible in that context. Need to find another way that fits company's culture way of working. Can mean: need to play company's rules like being in right meetings with right people, or even need to move into management career to influence decisions where it happens. Focus on achieving goals and overcoming challenges within scope of action first. Once in middle of progress, aim to influence culture as part of journey. Cultural change is part of process: Best way to influence company culture is part of succeeding in high stake business challenges. Shouldn't aim for highly disruptive new way of working, but "the adjacent possible." Example: If part of organization with top-down approach, secretive, oral-based communication, can start influencing by driving initiative making information available and written, but keeping approach top-down. Don't want to change too many ways of doing stuff at same time. When you succeed: (1) Achieve business objectives first, delivering company and customer value, (2) Create trust between all parties (high level leadership and teams), (3) Show that by doing X, Y, Z achieved objectives. Company's love success, we all try to repeat success. Next time face similar challenge, default approach will be new ways of working. When you fail: (1) Learn why failed - don't blame culture, understand why approach didn't succeed, then adapt methods and try new approaches, (2) Don't create high resistance to change due to disruptive new ways of working - can prevent any new improvement initiatives in future. Worst: people stop believing in change. Make it iterative: Don't try to change everything at once. Make small, incremental changes that fit within existing culture. As succeed with these changes, they become new default, gradually shifting culture. Aim for "adjacent possible" - changes one step away from where are, not massive disruptions. Reduces resistance, increases chances of success. Iterative approach: (1) Understand culture using 7 dimensions, (2) Identify smallest change moving toward goal, (3) Make that change within existing cultural constraints, (4) Succeed or learn from failure, (5) Repeat with next adjacent change. Key takeaways: Culture is how decisions made - understand first before trying to change. Use 7 dimensions to map organization culture. For middle management, follow culture first - aiming to drive cultural change directly is recipe for burnout. If approach doesn't fit, problem is your approach - find another way that fits. Cultural change happens through success - influence by succeeding in high-stake business challenges using new ways of working. Aim for adjacent possible - small incremental changes rather than disruptive transformations. Make it iterative - one step at a time, building on each success. Success creates trust and new defaults - when companies see success with new approaches, become default for future challenges. Failure is learning not blame - understand why approaches fail and adapt, don't blame culture. Avoid creating resistance - disruptive changes can prevent future improvement initiatives.

### Build vs Buy: Experimentation Platforms

- **Source:** Statsig Blog
- **Author:** Statsig
- **URL:** https://www.statsig.com/articles/build-vs-buy
- **Date:** Published on Statsig
- **Key Concepts:** Experimentation platforms, A/B testing infrastructure, build vs buy, total cost of ownership, opportunity cost, product experimentation, variant assignment, data processing, experiment analysis
- **Key Insight:** Framework for deciding whether to build an in-house experimentation platform or buy/rent one from external provider. Context: Companies like Bing, Google, LinkedIn increased experiments by order of magnitude after building platforms, run tens of thousands per year. Large tech companies have dedicated teams, but must decide based on business context. Two key questions: (1) What would it cost to build your own platform? (2) What can external platform deliver? Building your own requires investment in three areas: (1) Reliable core infrastructure to control and execute experiments, (2) Analysis framework to make sense of data and product decisions, (3) SDKs and tools to integrate with existing software development and decision-making processes. Core infrastructure has four components: (1) Experiment Control - persists and validates experiment specifications including iterations, parameters (start/end dates, control/variants, target users, sample sizes, configuration), enforces approval workflows, roles/permissions ensuring only approved experiments run, only authorized members start/stop. (2) Variant Assignment - decides how to assign user request to variant based on experiment specification and pseudo-random hash. Important considerations: random but deterministic assignment (users don't switch experiences), assigning users across multiple concurrent experiments ensuring consistent experience, keeping experiments isolated (one user not simultaneously in multiple conflicting experiments), delivering assignments without additional latency or hurting application availability. (3) Data Processing - reliable automated data processing critical for organizational trust. Steps: consolidate/sort/join logs from different sources, cleanse and enrich logs, compute metrics for segments/user dimensions, calculate p-values and confidence intervals. Critical: run A/A tests to ensure correctness, run basic health checks. (4) Experiment Analysis - enable everyone to easily read/interpret results. Separates valuable platform from one discarded quickly. Essential features: clear concise results highlighting relative change in metrics with statistical significance signals, must provide confidence intervals (platforms that don't are amusing at best, misleading/damaging at worst), reveal all relevant metrics not just experiment-tailored one, enable slicing across user dimensions/segments, use lower p-values to filter most significant metrics, per metric views of all experiment results to see most impactful experiments. APIs and SDKs: Experiment configurations must reach applications via APIs/SDKs. Considerations: support for client-side or server-side production decisioning, support for all languages teams choose, SDKs resilient to network flakiness, API versioning carefully considered with existing SDK usage. Buying/Renting considerations: (1) Core Functionality - delivers needed functionality? Optimized for front-end or back-end? Works for web and mobile? (2) SDK Support - supports SDK in needed language? (3) Performance - do SDK calls slow application? (4) Data Ingestion - can ingest from different sources to avoid logging with multiple services, join data for richer analysis? (5) Near Real-time Results - need near-real time to quickly detect/stop bad experiments? (6) Randomization Unit - allows randomization units like sessions/workspaces in addition to users? (7) Metrics - computes all required metrics? Do metrics reconcile with other company sources? (8) Health & Audit - automatically surfaces health/system metrics to proactively identify issues? Provides approval workflows for team reviews? Provides audit log of changes? (9) Access Control - allows controlling who has permissions to start/stop experiments? Building vs Buying: Estimate how many experiments organization would run assuming everyone embraces experimentation. As experiments scale, infrastructure becomes increasingly complex. To decide, consider TCO and opportunity cost of building in-house vs functional needs, reliability, scalability, costs of external service. Total Cost of Ownership for in-house: (1) Development - requires top-notch infrastructure engineers for high availability/performance, dedicated data scientists/data engineers for reliable trustworthy data processing/computations/results. (2) Operations & Support - team needs on-call rotation for uptime SLAs, support inquiries/tickets from all company users. (3) Infrastructure - must be redundant/resilient with automated scaling for stable trusted service, include monitoring/operational tooling, near-real time results increases complexity/cost further. (4) SDKs for Everyone - each team requires client/server SDKs in preferred language, platform team needs engineers familiar with language nuances to build/support SDKs over time. Opportunity Cost: Building in-house comes at expense of engineers/dollars that could invest in core product/functionality users want. Good experimentation platform accelerates innovation pace. If slows client teams, they won't use it and initial investment wasted. If decide to build, must invest in team/infrastructure/SDKs to stay ahead of all client teams' needs. External service advantage: Assuming external service meets functional requirements, buying may offer faster innovation pace than in-house. External services committed to innovating on behalf of all customers and setting industry standards for scalability, performance, security, governance. Means enjoy leading innovation pace in experimentation by default. When building might be better: Some cases building better if external services don't meet functional requirements. Example: Software deeply integrated with hardware (e.g., Roku) may need unique/customized way to deploy/control experiments. Summary: External service helps focus on driving car rather than assembling platform parts. For flexibility, consider service with free tier and easy onboarding to see value of simple A/B tests without contracts. Get something that works day one, most importantly provides transparent pricing so only get what need.

### Using Cultural Survey Data

- **Source:** Irrational Exuberance (lethain.com)
- **Author:** Will Larson (Author of "An Elegant Puzzle," "Staff Engineer," "The Engineering Executive's Primer," and "Crafting Engineering Strategy")
- **URL:** https://lethain.com/using-cultural-survey-data/
- **Date:** 2025
- **Original Source:** This is an unedited chapter from O'Reilly's "The Engineering Executive's Primer"
- **Key Concepts:** Cultural surveys, employee feedback, survey analysis, data literacy, executive accountability, organizational culture, team trust, survey action planning, survey frequency
- **Key Insight:** How to effectively read, analyze, and take action on cultural survey results to build trust and address team concerns. Context: Almost every scaled company runs cultural surveys (vendors like CultureAmp or Lattice, or in-house tools). Surveys ask about experience working in company, cross-team collaboration, compensation, etc. Results tallied, reports generated for each manager about their team, executive team gets company-wide report. Senior leaders usually don't spend much time on survey results, but they're great lens into data literacy and how leaders prioritize limited resources to address broad problem space. Valuable mechanism for earning credibility by following through to address concerns. Reading results: Quick advice - spend couple hours digesting results, focus more on absolute scores than relative (high score still high even if relatively down), identify all issues not just first couple noticed. Formalized 10-step approach: (1) Check access - ensure whole company access not just engineering report, executive team should see full results (transparency helps gel executive team, see cross-functional patterns like Engineering and Product both frustrated, identify areas other functions frustrated with Engineering). (2) Create private document for notes - screen capture data raising issues, add commentary, staging ground not final product. (3) Understand population sizes - build intuition for statistical significance (changes significant in org of 2,000 but technical program manager team of 7, extreme change probably not significant). Be skeptical of narratives - survey tools incentivized to produce narratives even if weak, cautiously skeptical of analysis lacking statistical significance, use comments/follow-ups rather than raw numbers for non-significant findings. (4) Skim and categorize - group into three categories: things to celebrate, things to proactively address, things to acknowledge. Capture highlights in addition to concerns (never want purely negative update). (5) Check previous investments - how did you perform on areas identified after last survey? Highlight success, form hypothesis if didn't have impact. (6) Focus on extremes - highest and lowest absolute ratings, have they changed, did you expect them to change? (7) Focus on rapid changes - ratings changing fastest, what's driving rapid shift? (8) Compare across cohorts - different managers, tenure, gender, location. (9) Read every comment - copy most relevant to notes, what subjective feedback changes interpretation, what comments to quote when sharing summary. (10) Discuss with peer - spend hour with peer (e.g., head of product) after they've reviewed their report. Identify connected themes across orgs, find gaps in analysis. Don't do with direct report (messy discussing peers, avoid thorniest issues). Taking action: Problem - teams spend time running/analyzing but never use analysis for anything (frustrates team, trains them to ignore future surveys). Other extreme - earnest leaders commit to fixing everything (team loves initially, but six months later little progress). Middle path - identify few important areas where can make real progress, actually do work. Standard pattern: (1) Address acute issues immediately - manager whose team in open revolt, ethical issue raised. (2) Select 2-3 investment areas - use analysis notes, invest until next survey (e.g., increasing representation for Staff-plus engineers in key decision making forums, increasing sense of belonging for remote employees). (3) Create shareable document - edit notes and investment areas into document comfortable sharing, err on transparent but optimistic transparency (things relatively down often still absolutely high, recent trend down important but still significantly higher than historical averages). (4) Review with multiple stakeholders - direct reports (team meeting), People/HR partner, peers on executive team, 2-3 trusted individual contributors within Engineering (more sensitive to issues easier to miss as executive). (5) Incorporate feedback carefully - reviewers always find something important missed, listen carefully even if doesn't initially resonate. (6) Define explicit, verifiable actions - abstract commitments don't build trust, ambiguous whether followed through. (7) Share and discuss - share document with organization, schedule meeting to discuss findings and priorities with entire engineering organization (often repurpose Engineering Q&A sessions). (8) Follow up monthly - progress against action items (often include in weekly updates). (9) Mention improvements next survey - team takes survey more seriously if know you took action last time, helps score higher on questions about making improvements, new hires won't know if improvements made unless do this. Outcome: Straightforward process builds team trust and stronger culture. Executives rarely held accountable unless expose themselves to accountability. Valuable opportunity to hold yourself accountable to team. As hold yourself accountable, becomes easier to hold team accountable. Questions to ask: Executive teams debate adding questions every time survey about to run. While important to ensure good coverage, generally default questions good enough, not worthwhile to change. Two types of data: (1) Ratings on questions - valuable because can compare across cohorts, more useful seeing trends over time. Expensive to change - each change wipes out historical context, changes from default mean can't compare against industry benchmarks. Recommend against changing unless folks frequently confused (e.g., confusion between "the Management Team" vs "your manager" - executive team refers to itself as "Management Team" but wider company doesn't know, confusion invalidates results so change for clarity more valuable than preserving continuity). (2) Free-form responses - provide subjective context. Relatively cheap to change - no history to wipe out. However, if folks have something to say, they'll say it anywhere even if question somewhat unrelated. Adding more free-form questions doesn't change that, so as long as handful of open-ended questions, don't worry about adding more. Conclusion: Worth evaluating questions before first rollout, but repeated arguments about changing questions is executive bikeshedding. Starting and frequency: When to start - small organizations with genuine relationship with every team member won't get much from survey. As organization grows, become more useful. Dunbar's number (roughly 150) good point to ensure running surveys. Frequency - most run twice a year, reasonable amount. Principled way to determine: consider how much time willing to invest addressing issues raised. If struggling to address concerns from biannual surveys, doesn't make sense to run more frequently (annoy folks, complain about running another survey before addressing previously raised concerns). It can be uncomfortable: Sometimes embarrassed by scores. Called out for not paying attention to team's priorities. Tried to prioritize remote working experience, regressed rather than improved. Had below average scores and bad scores. Approach: Take moment, be uncomfortable with it. It means you care! Then lean into feedback, use to motivate addressing issues. Key takeaways: (1) Cultural surveys valuable lenses - reveal data literacy and how leaders prioritize limited resources. (2) Reading results requires structure - systematic approach: check access, create notes, understand population sizes, categorize, compare, read comments, discuss with peers. (3) Focus on absolute scores - high score still high even if relatively down. (4) Be skeptical of narratives - survey tools incentivized to produce narratives, cautiously skeptical especially of analysis lacking statistical significance. (5) Take action on 2-3 areas - don't commit to fixing everything but don't ignore results. (6) Be transparent and optimistic - share broadly but frame optimistically. (7) Define explicit, verifiable actions - abstract commitments don't build trust. (8) Follow up monthly - regular updates build trust. (9) Don't change questions unnecessarily - preserve historical context and industry benchmarking. (10) Start around 150 people (Dunbar's number). (11) Run twice a year typically - frequency should match capacity to address concerns. (12) Embrace discomfort - bad scores uncomfortable but means you care, use as motivation.

### Never Rewrite Code From Scratch

- **Source:** Joel on Software (joelonsoftware.com)
- **Author:** Joel Spolsky (Co-founder of Fog Creek Software and Stack Overflow, CEO of Stack Overflow 2010-2019)
- **URL:** https://www.joelonsoftware.com/2000/04/06/things-you-should-never-do-part-i/
- **Date:** 2025
- **Key Concepts:** Code rewriting, refactoring, technical debt, software architecture, legacy code, code maintenance, incremental improvement, software development mistakes
- **Key Insight:** Rewriting code from scratch is "the single worst strategic mistake that any software company can make." Famous warning based on Netscape 6.0 rewrite disaster (3 years between releases, market share plummeted). Historical examples: Borland (dBase for Windows, Quattro Pro rewrite), Microsoft (Pyramid project for Word for Windows - shut down). Why programmers want to rewrite: Programmers are architects who want to bulldoze and rebuild, not do incremental renovation. Fundamental law of programming: "It's harder to read code than to write it" - explains why code always seems messy. Why code seems messy: Those "hairs" on functions are actually bug fixes - fixes for edge cases discovered through weeks of real-world usage (Nancy's bug, low memory conditions, floppy disk yanks, old Windows 95 compatibility). Each bug fix represents weeks of discovery plus days of reproduction/fixing. Cost of rewriting: (1) Throw away all knowledge, collected bug fixes, years of programming work. (2) Throw away market leadership - give competitors 2-3 years (long time in software). (3) Can't react to market - stuck shipping old code, can't make strategic changes. (4) Waste money writing code that already exists. Why old code is valuable: Old code has been used, tested, bugs found and fixed. Software doesn't rust. Idea that new code is better is patently absurd. Netscape code may have been "bad" but worked on many real-world systems. What's actually wrong with "messy" code: Three types of problems: (1) Architectural problems - code not factored correctly, can be solved incrementally by moving code, refactoring, changing interfaces. Example: Juno project rearchitected over several months without throwing away code. (2) Inefficiency - only affects small part, can optimize or rewrite just that part. "When optimizing for speed, 1% of work gets 99% of bang." (3) Ugliness - solve with macros, find/replace, not by rewriting. Example: FuckedString data type, underscore vs "m_" conventions. The rebuild fallacy: No reason to believe you'll do better - probably different team, will make same mistakes plus new ones. "Build one to throw away" mantra: Dangerous for large-scale commercial applications. Fine for experimental code (ripping up last week's function, refactoring class) but folly for whole programs. Key insight: Refactor incrementally, don't rewrite. Preserve knowledge while fixing problems.

### Technical Hype and Cold Showers

- **Source:** Awesome Cold Showers (GitHub Repository)
- **Author/Curator:** Hillel Wayne (hwayne)
- **URL:** https://github.com/hwayne/awesome-cold-showers
- **Date:** Repository created and maintained (continually updated)
- **Key Concepts:** Technical hype, over-engineering, reality checks, microservices, big data systems, scaling, performance benchmarking, technical decision-making
- **Key Insight:** Curated collection of rigorous and respectful "cold showers" on overhyped technical topics. Premise: It's great when people get excited, but sometimes they get too excited - these are reminders to stay grounded. Not meant to dismiss enthusiasm, but to provide reality checks. For startups: Following technical hype can lead to over-engineering, premature optimization, choosing complex solutions for problems you don't have, wasting time/resources. Key examples: (1) Microservices - Five fallacies of why microservices solve problems monoliths have, shows either monoliths don't have those problems or microservices make them worse. Lesson: Start with monolith, microservices solve scaling/organizational problems you likely don't have yet, add significant operational complexity. (2) Scalability COST paper - Benchmarking graph-processing algorithms on 128-core clusters vs single-threaded 2014 Macbook Pro, laptop consistently wins (sometimes by order of magnitude). Lesson: "If using big data system for yourself, see if faster than your laptop. If building big data system for others, see it's faster than my laptop." Many startups over-invest in distributed systems when single server suffices. Scale when actually need to scale, not before. (3) Scaling SQLite to 4M QPS - Expensify found single bare-metal server faster and cheaper than x1e.32xlarge EC2 instance, avoided sharding complexity. Lesson: Don't assume need to scale out or use cloud services - sometimes single powerful server is faster/cheaper, especially avoiding sharding complexity. (4) Agile Methods critique - Review showing some practices (replacing requirements with user stories, lack of proper specification) harmful long run. Lesson: Agile good but imperfect, don't adopt dogma uncritically, some practices cause problems. (5) VM Warmup - Many benchmarks slow down over time, never stabilize, not repeatable due to non-determinism, warmup time important but usually ignored/reported inaccurately. Lesson: Don't trust performance claims without verifying in your context, many improvements don't hold up under realistic conditions. (6) Web Framework Benchmarks - Actual hard data of various combinations under various tasks. Lesson: Look for actual benchmark data not marketing claims, raw numbers in isolation don't tell whole story, need context and trade-offs. (7) Static vs Dynamic Typing - Review of literature showing solid research inconclusive about whether static typing reduces bugs, conclusive research had methodological issues. (8) Formal Verification - Literature review showing formal methods hard to learn, extremely expensive, often miss critical bugs. Three formally verified systems had critical correctness bugs (at system boundaries, not in protocols). (9) Identifier Naming - Research shows no difference in debugging time/quality between abbreviated vs full-word names. Eye-tracking suggests underscore may be processed faster than camelCase but research not definitive. (10) Go Concurrency - Despite claims Go's concurrency easier/less buggy, empirical study found plenty of concurrency bugs in popular Go software (Docker, Kubernetes, gRPC), more than half caused by Go-specific problems. Pattern: Question hype - do we actually have the problem this solves? What's actual evidence not just marketing? What are trade-offs/complexity costs? What if we started simpler? Stay grounded - best decisions based on understanding actual problems, evidence not hype, simplicity over complexity, incremental improvements over rewrites, measured results over isolated benchmarks. Key takeaways: Microservices often solve problems you don't have (start with monolith), big data systems may be slower than laptop (measure before assuming need distributed), scaling out isn't always better (single server can be faster/cheaper), Agile has problems (good but imperfect, don't adopt dogma), performance claims need verification (benchmarks misleading, test in context), question all hype (enthusiasm can lead astray), start simple (add complexity later if needed), measure don't assume (test actual solutions in context), evidence over marketing (rigorous studies not vendor claims), complexity has costs (every technology adds operational/cognitive overhead).

- **Key Insight:** The top indicator of a successful team isn't tenure, seniority or salary levels. It's psychological safety. Based on Project Aristotle findings from Google. Five warning signs of unsafe teams: (1) "If I take a chance, and screw up, it will be held against me" - mistakes held against people, never try to innovate again without explicit permission. (2) "Our team has a strong sense of culture, and it's hard for new people to join" - defending reputation more important than new hires, team/manager don't have your back. (3) "My team is slow to offer help to people who are struggling" - no formal training, no signals it's OK to learn, develop impostor syndrome. (4) "Using my unique skills and talents come second to the objectives of the team" - skills go unused, grow frustrated, drop proposals without discussion. (5) "It's uncomfortable to have open, honest conversations about our team's sensitive issues" - can't discuss root causes, look for new job. Unsafe teams can deliver short-term but eventually break or underperform drastically because people can't introduce change. How to build: (1) Balance culture with openness to change - respect culture but be open to changing it as needed (team working from home needs to change for interns, on-call culture may need professionalization). (2) Balance work people love with work company needs - great managers expand team remit to use engineers' skills rather than just moving poor fits. (3) Make respect part of culture - "the standard you walk past is the standard you accept" (David Morrison), challenge disrespect immediately, politely, in front of everyone. Examples: assumptions about technical ability (female PM with PhD), mocking previous positions, quiet engineers talked over. (4) Make space for people to take chances - innovation time/wiggle weeks (Intercom's 6-week sprints), but not only then, improve ideas rather than discounting them. (5) Make it obvious when team is doing well - celebrate visible success (Post-It goals), celebrate glorious failure (Google storage SRE example - learned more in 3 days than 3 months, toast what was learned). (6) Clear communication and explicit expectations - build systems (SLOs) rather than yelling, ask for specific commitments, make post-mortems learning opportunities not witch-hunts, set expectations upfront. Action steps: (1) Give team short survey, share results, (2) Discuss what "Safety" means, share when felt "unsafe", (3) Build culture of respect & clear communication starting with your actions. Treat psychological safety as key business metric (as important as revenue, cost of sales, uptime). The Karen story: Graduate makes optimization that causes outage, labeled "irresponsible", no one defends her, no training offered, skills go unused, can't discuss problems, leaves company with design that could have saved product. Key takeaways: (1) Psychological safety is #1 indicator of team success, (2) Unsafe teams break eventually, (3) Five warning signs, (4) Balance culture with openness to change, (5) Balance work people love with work company needs, (6) Make respect part of culture, (7) Make space for people to take chances, (8) Celebrate both success and glorious failure, (9) Clear communication and explicit expectations, (10) Treat as business metric.

- **Key Insight:** Documented characteristics and habits of highest-performing teams based on direct experience working with hundreds of developers. Process experiments possible when high safety (example: Feedback Week - one-week process where everyone randomly assigned to collect feedback for another, created self-reinforcing positive feedback cycle). True psychological safety goes deeper than training - requires understanding invisible social structures, social privilege, how they affect participation, otherwise microaggressions/stereotype threat/survivorship bias manifest. (2) Good hygiene practices - Woods Theorem: "As complexity of system increases, accuracy of any single agent's model decreases rapidly." Can't have comprehensive mental model of large systems, rely on artifacts left by previous developers. Good hygiene = taking extra time to record present-day context: descriptive commit messages (at minimum verb, some teams require issue numbers), intention-revealing class/method names following conventions, unit tests with helpful descriptions/realistic variable names, keeping communication in tracking issues not Slack DMs. About empathy - for teammates (cleaner artifacts = less context-switching) and future self (3am pager pay-off). (3) Active redistribution of "experience points" - RPG analogy (Fire Emblem/Pokemon): level up characters evenly, don't let high-level characters hog experience from low/mid-level. In software: every new piece of work spreads domain context and confidence, don't constantly deploy only senior developers to tackle difficult stories, healthy teams engage in context redistribution so less experienced engineers gain valuable experience points. No central tactician role (managers/PMs don't count - not enough visibility/context, bad to centralize on one person). Raises productivity and morale if everyone feels somewhat equipped to tackle challenges, or knows can ask for help. (4) Communicating generously - Robustness principle (Nat Friedman): "Be conservative in what you send, be liberal in what you accept." Principle of charity. Codebar mentor training: "Assume every student has limited information, but infinite intelligence." Assume peer is competent/intelligent/reasonable asking question because lacking context, has already attempted to procure on own. Frustrating to have context/experience "rounded down" constantly (gendered element). Examples: Lack of generosity - assume questioner doesn't know basics, provide articles on fundamentals, creates friction. Generosity - check assumptions first ("I'm guessing you're asking about... Stop me if wrong"), gets to solution faster, fewer words, no unnecessary friction, united in slaying uncertainty rather than jousting over knowledge levels. Key takeaways: (1) High psychological safety enables process experiments, (2) Goes deeper than training (social structures/privilege), (3) Observable behaviors (retros with real issues, struggle timebox, value separated from output, more PTO), (4) Good hygiene is empathy, (5) Hygiene practices (descriptive commits, intention-revealing names, helpful tests, context in issues), (6) Redistribute experience points, (7) No central tactician, (8) Communicate generously (assume competence), (9) Check assumptions first, (10) Generosity creates positive cycles.

What to teach: how to focus. Two areas to practice showing: figuring out what's important, and going home. Practical tips for new managers: When newish manager and haven't figured out tricks to do job effectively, might need to work more hours for little while - that's ok. Put chat status as "away" in off hours. Take vacation and don't answer email during that time. Constantly ask yourself same questions you ask team: Can I do this faster? Do I need to be doing this at all? What is the value I'm providing with this work? The virtuous cycle: Laziness and impatience. We focus so we can go home, and we encourage going home because it forces us to constantly focus. This is how great teams scale. Key takeaways: (1) Laziness, Impatience, Hubris as virtues (from Larry Wall), (2) Applied to processes not people, (3) Two areas to practice (figuring out what's important, going home), (4) Be impatient to figure out what's important, (5) "Faster" doesn't mean more hours (same value in less total time), (6) Go home (stop emailing at all hours), (7) Prevent team burnout, (8) Practical tips (queue emails, set away status, take vacation, ask yourself same questions), (9) Automation to make job easier, (10) Virtuous cycle (focus so we can go home, going home forces focus, how great teams scale).

### Great Developers Are Raised, Not Hired

- **Source:** sizovs.net
- **Author:** Eduard Sizov
- **URL:** https://sizovs.net/mentoring/
- **Date:** Not specified
- **Key Concepts:** Mentoring, hiring strategy, talent development, team building, impostor syndrome, growth mindset, raw diamonds, mentoring capacity, pair programming, career development
- **Key Insight:** A counterintuitive approach to hiring: Instead of searching for the "best talent" that may not exist, companies should focus on building mentoring capacity and raising developers. Hire for attitude, teach technical skills. Core problem: Every company is looking for "best talent" (ideal candidate with all technical skills, culture fit, performs with little supervision), but best developers hired were far from ideal - lacked technical skills, didn't know testing/agile, didn't believe in themselves, had impostor syndrome, didn't sound convincing in interviews. Companies create complicated multi-step interview processes that filter out "not good enough" candidates, rarely give opportunity to candidates spoiled by shitty companies/bad influence, forget some candidates weren't lucky enough to meet inspiring mentor or work in supportive team - "broken toys" not welcome. We prefer hiring self-confident rockstars with solid track record because we fail to create environments where people can grow professionally, develop good habits, become rockstars. Skilled developers don't have time for mentoring (busy coding/architecture), mentoring not part of hiring/delivery processes. Companies spend millions on TA departments, recruiters, promo websites/videos struggling to find best talent, but have talent search problem because searching for best engineers instead of raising them - "everyone is fishing but not much fish left, recruiting more fishers doesn't help, the pond is empty." Solution: Build mentoring capacity - experienced developers should grow less experienced colleagues through pair programming, continuous feedback, career advice, suggest books/blogs/videos, encourage meetups/conferences/workshops. Mentoring requires significant time commitment and excellent communication skills, but is best way to get followers and boost authority/reputation - "your mentees are your fan club" (will support and promote you for rest of life). By building mentoring capacity, can hire newcomers, broken toys, impostors and turn them into loyal high-quality workforce. Shifting focus from assessing to mentoring simplifies interviewing process - instead of trying to predict future performance through tough questions and "nine circles of hell," hire candidates with growth mindset and grow them. Raw diamond analogy: Companies look for polished diamonds and create hiring processes that throw raw diamonds in trash bin. Instead, learn to polish them - create environments where people with eagerness and capacity to learn can become great developers. Four ways: (1) Take money/energy/time spent on recruiting and invest in teaching best developers mentoring skills, give time for mentoring and free them from routine tasks, (2) Adjust interview process and give chance to candidates not good enough yet but eager to learn with growth mindset (raw diamonds), (3) Relax hard requirements in job ads to avoid filtering out impostors (remember most women apply only where they completely fit requirements), (4) Large companies consider creating bootcamp for complete beginners. Core principle: Escape crazy hiring race by creating environment where experienced developers mentor less experienced developers. "Hire for attitude, and teach technical skills. Great developers are raised, not hired." Be the company that says "we are ~~hiring~~ mentoring." Key takeaways: (1) Best developers hired were far from ideal candidates, (2) Companies filter out broken toys, (3) We fail to create environments where people grow, (4) The pond is empty (everyone fishing, not much fish), (5) Build mentoring capacity, (6) Mentoring benefits mentors too (authority, reputation, fan club), (7) Raw diamonds analogy (polish instead of throwing away), (8) Shift from assessing to mentoring (simplify interviewing), (9) Four ways to build mentoring culture, (10) Hire for attitude, teach technical skills.
